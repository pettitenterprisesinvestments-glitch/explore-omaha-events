# The Legal Structure of Your Business Is Either Protecting You or Exposing You

Most small business owners choose a business structure once — usually at the beginning, usually quickly, and usually without a full understanding of what they are actually deciding.

They form an LLC because someone told them to. Or they stay a sole proprietor because setting up an entity felt complicated. Or they incorporate because an attorney suggested it without fully explaining what it means for their specific situation.

The structure of your business is not a formality. It is the legal wall between your business liabilities and your personal assets. And for most small business owners, that wall has significant gaps they have never been told about.

---

## What a Business Structure Actually Does

When you operate as a sole proprietor — which is the default if you have not formed a separate legal entity — there is no legal distinction between you and your business. Every liability your business incurs is your personal liability. A lawsuit against your business is a lawsuit against you personally. A business debt is your personal debt.

This is not a technicality. It means your personal bank accounts, your home, your vehicle, and your personal savings are all potentially reachable by a creditor or plaintiff if your business is sued or cannot pay its debts.

Forming a legal entity — an LLC, a corporation, or another structure — creates a separation between your personal assets and your business liabilities. This separation is called limited liability protection, and it is the primary reason most business owners form entities in the first place.

The catch is that the protection is not automatic just because the entity exists.

---

## Why the Wall Gets Torn Down

Limited liability protection can be lost — a legal concept called "piercing the corporate veil." When a court pierces the veil, it looks through the entity structure and holds the owner personally liable as if no entity existed at all.

The most common reasons this happens:

Commingling personal and business funds. If you are running personal expenses through your business account, or depositing business income into your personal account, you are signaling that the entity is not actually operating as a separate legal person. Courts have consistently found this to be grounds for piercing.

Failing to follow formalities. LLCs and corporations have legal requirements — annual filings, registered agent maintenance, operating agreements or bylaws, and in some states, annual meetings or resolutions. Ignoring these requirements can undermine the entity's legitimacy.

Undercapitalization. If a business is started without adequate capital to meet its reasonably anticipated obligations, courts have in some cases found that the owner should have known the entity would be unable to meet its debts — and held them personally liable.

Personal guarantees. Many business loans and leases require a personal guarantee from the owner, which means you are personally liable for that obligation regardless of your business structure. This is worth knowing before you sign.

---

## Choosing the Right Structure for Your Situation

The question of which entity type is right for a specific business depends on factors including the number of owners, the nature of the business, the state in which it operates, and the tax treatment the owner prefers.

An LLC is the most common choice for small business owners because it offers liability protection with relatively minimal formality requirements and flexible tax treatment. A single-member LLC is taxed as a sole proprietor by default. A multi-member LLC is taxed as a partnership. Either can elect to be taxed as an S corporation if that produces better tax outcomes.

An S corporation is often used by business owners who want to reduce self-employment tax liability by paying themselves a reasonable salary and taking additional income as distributions. This can produce meaningful tax savings for businesses with consistent profitability — but the setup and ongoing requirements are more complex than a standard LLC.

A C corporation is less common for small businesses but is the required structure for certain types of investment, particularly venture-backed companies. The double taxation issue — profits taxed at the corporate level and again when distributed to shareholders — makes it less favorable for most small business owners.

The right answer is specific to your situation. A conversation with a qualified attorney or CPA who understands your business is worth far more than a general recommendation.

---

## What to Review Right Now

If you already have a business entity, these are the questions worth asking today:

Is your operating agreement or bylaws current, and does it reflect how the business actually operates?

Do you have a separate business bank account, and is it being used exclusively for business transactions?

Are your annual state filings current, and is your registered agent information up to date?

Have you reviewed any contracts you have signed for personal guarantee language?

Are there any business liabilities — loans, leases, vendor agreements — where you are exposed personally that you did not fully understand when you signed?

If the answer to any of these is uncertain, that is worth a conversation with an attorney who handles business matters.
