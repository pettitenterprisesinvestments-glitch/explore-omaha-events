# When Someone Steals Your Identity: What Happens Next and How to Fight Back

Identity theft does not feel like a crime until it feels like a catastrophe.

It starts quietly. A credit card application you did not submit. A collection notice for an account you never opened. A tax return that was already filed under your Social Security number when you try to file yours. A medical bill from a provider you have never visited.

By the time most people realize their identity has been compromised, the damage has already spread across multiple accounts, multiple institutions, and sometimes multiple years.

What most victims do not know is that the process of recovery is navigable — but it requires knowing the right steps, in the right order, with the right documentation.

---

## How Identity Theft Actually Works

The popular image of identity theft involves a hacker and a stolen password. That is one vector. But identity theft happens through many channels that have nothing to do with cybersecurity.

Mail theft. A stolen piece of mail containing a pre-approved credit offer or a financial statement is enough to open new accounts in your name.

Data breaches. When a company you have an account with is breached, your personal information — name, address, Social Security number, date of birth — may be sold on the dark web and used to open credit accounts, file fraudulent tax returns, or apply for government benefits.

Social engineering. Phishing emails, fake customer service calls, and fraudulent websites designed to look like legitimate institutions capture credentials and personal details from millions of people every year.

Insider access. Employees of financial institutions, healthcare providers, and government agencies have access to personal records that can be misused.

The source of the theft often cannot be identified. The impact, however, always has to be addressed.

---

## The First 48 Hours

If you believe your identity has been stolen or compromised, the first 48 hours matter more than any other period in the recovery process.

Place a fraud alert with one of the three major credit bureaus — Equifax, Experian, or TransUnion. When you place a fraud alert with one, they are required to notify the other two. A fraud alert tells creditors to take extra steps to verify identity before opening new accounts in your name.

Consider a credit freeze. A credit freeze is more restrictive than a fraud alert — it prevents new credit from being opened in your name entirely until you lift it. It is free to place and lift at each bureau and is the strongest protection against new fraudulent accounts.

Pull your credit reports from all three bureaus immediately. Look for accounts you do not recognize, inquiries you did not initiate, and addresses or employers you have never been associated with. Every item that is not yours is a potential fraudulent account that needs to be disputed.

File a report with the FTC at IdentityTheft.gov. This generates an official Identity Theft Report that you will use throughout the recovery process. It is not a police report, but it carries legal weight with creditors and credit bureaus.

File a police report with your local law enforcement. Some creditors and institutions require a police report number to process identity theft claims. Getting this report filed early protects you throughout what can be a months-long process.

---

## Disputing Fraudulent Accounts

Every fraudulent account on your credit report can be disputed directly with the credit bureau that is reporting it. The Fair Credit Reporting Act gives you the right to dispute inaccurate information, and the bureau has 30 days to investigate and respond.

In parallel, contact the fraud department of each creditor directly. Request that the fraudulent accounts be closed and that any associated negative marks be removed from your credit report. Send all communications in writing and keep copies of everything.

This process works — but it takes time, persistence, and documentation. The paper trail you build in the early days of discovery is the evidence you will rely on throughout.

## When the Damage Goes Beyond Credit

Identity theft does not always stay in the financial lane.

Tax identity theft occurs when someone files a tax return in your name using your Social Security number to claim a refund. The IRS has a specific process for addressing this, including an Identity Protection PIN that prevents future fraudulent filings under your number. But resolving a fraudulent tax return can take months and requires specific documentation.

Medical identity theft occurs when someone uses your information to receive medical care or prescription drugs. The resulting fraudulent medical records can affect your own future medical care and insurance. Correcting medical records requires working directly with healthcare providers and insurance companies — a process that is more complex and less standardized than credit dispute processes.

Employment identity theft occurs when someone uses your Social Security number for employment. This can result in income being attributed to you that affects your taxes, your benefits, and your financial records.

Each of these requires a different process and often the involvement of different agencies.
