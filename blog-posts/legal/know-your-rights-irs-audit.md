# What to Do the Day You Get a Letter From the IRS

Most people have a visceral reaction to seeing the IRS return address on an envelope. The heart rate goes up. The worst-case scenarios start running. And a significant number of people set the letter aside and try not to think about it for a few days.

That last part is the one that creates the most problems.

An IRS notice does not get better with time. It does not resolve itself. And the options available to you on day one are almost always better than the options available to you after weeks of delay.

Here is what the letter actually means — and what to do about it.

---

## Most IRS Notices Are Not Audits

The first thing worth knowing is that the majority of IRS notices are not audit letters. They are automated notices generated when the IRS's systems identify a discrepancy, a missing document, a balance due, or a question about a specific line on a return.

Common notices include:

A CP2000 — which means the IRS received income information from an employer, bank, or other source that does not match what was reported on your return. This is a proposed adjustment, not a final assessment. You have the right to respond and dispute it.

A CP14 — which means there is a balance due. This is simply a bill. It does not mean fraud. It does not mean criminal investigation. It means the IRS believes you owe money and wants payment.

A CP501 or CP503 — which are follow-up reminders if a balance has not been addressed. These escalate in urgency but are still at the administrative stage.

An LT11 or CP90 — which are final notices before levy. These are more serious and require immediate attention.

The letter itself will identify the notice type. Reading it carefully — or having someone who knows tax law read it — is the essential first step.

---

## What Not to Do

Do not ignore it. Even a notice that turns out to be incorrect does not go away because you did not respond. The IRS will continue the process regardless. Deadlines pass. Options narrow. Penalties accumulate.

Do not call the IRS without understanding what you are dealing with first. The IRS phone lines are staffed by agents who are doing their job — which is to collect money the IRS believes is owed. Calling without understanding your position, your rights, and your options is not advisable.

Do not assume the notice is correct. The IRS makes errors. Income gets misattributed. Documents get misread. Deductions get disallowed incorrectly. Every notice has a response and appeals process for exactly this reason.

Do not pay immediately just to make it go away if you believe the notice is wrong. Payment in some circumstances can be interpreted as agreement with the underlying assessment, which complicates any subsequent dispute.

---

## What an Audit Actually Involves

If the letter you received is an actual audit notice — a request for information, documentation, or an in-person or correspondence examination — the rules are different and the stakes are higher.

An IRS audit is a formal examination of your tax return and the records that support it. It can be conducted by mail, at an IRS office, or in some cases at your home or business. The scope can be limited to a single item or expansive across multiple years.

You have rights during an audit that many taxpayers are not aware of:

You have the right to representation. You do not have to face an IRS audit alone or speak to the IRS directly. A CPA, an enrolled agent, or a tax attorney can represent you and speak on your behalf.

You have the right to appeal any finding you disagree with — first through the IRS's own appeals process, and if necessary through the U.S. Tax Court.

You have the right to know why you are being audited and what documentation is being requested. The IRS is required to be specific about what it needs.
