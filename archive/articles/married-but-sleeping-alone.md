Have you ever reached across the bed in the dark, expecting to find the person you married, and found nothing but a cold, empty sheet and a silence so complete that you lay there wondering whether you are even really married at all?

For so many couples raising little ones, it begins as the most innocent thing in the world. The baby will not settle, or the toddler is frightened of the dark, and so one of you lies down beside them just to help them drift off. Just for a few minutes. But a few minutes is all it takes, because lying still in a dark, quiet room is the surest way for a worn-out parent to fall asleep too. And so one of you wakes at two in the morning in a narrow bed surrounded by stuffed animals, while the other wakes alone in the master bedroom, marooned on the wide, cold acreage of a bed built for two.

If we are honest, it is not all heartbreak, and that is part of the trouble. The husband lying by himself will admit, a little guiltily, that he is sleeping better than he has in years. No small foot wedged into his spine. No three in the morning blanket tug-of-war. And, bless her heart, no snoring rattling the headboard like a freight train rolling straight through the bedroom. Some quiet part of him has started to enjoy the peace. But a marriage rarely ends in one dramatic moment. More often it drifts apart one comfortable, well-rested night at a time.

And let no one pretend this only runs in one direction. There are just as many homes where it is the wife left alone in the big bed while her husband dozes off in the recliner with the game still murmuring, or sprawls beside the toddler with his phone glowing in his hand until midnight. There are wives who have quietly come to prefer the warm little body of a child who still reaches for them over the husband who stopped reaching long ago, who find it easier to curl up in the nursery than to face the cool, awkward distance that has settled onto their own side of the bed. Both of you can become complicit in the slow goodnight. Both of you can start choosing the easier comfort over the harder closeness, and then quietly blame the other for the space it leaves behind.

Here is the first lie this season loves to tell: it is only temporary, so it does not really matter. The children are little, the days are exhausting, and you promise yourselves you will find your way back to each other once they are older. But a marriage is not built in the grand future moments we keep deferring. It is built in the thousand ordinary nights you are living right now. A bed slept in alone, night after night, does not stay a simple matter of logistics. It slowly becomes a matter of the heart. The plain truth is that the way you spend your ordinary nights is, in large part, the way you are building, or unbuilding, your marriage. This season matters precisely because it is the one you are actually in.

And here is the second lie, the quieter and more pious one: that wanting your spouse back beside you is selfish, and that a truly good parent, a truly sacrificial Christian, would simply endure the empty bed without a word. But Scripture never asks a husband and wife to lay their oneness on the altar of good parenting. "Therefore a man shall leave his father and his mother and hold fast to his wife, and they shall become one flesh" (Genesis 2:24). The marriage came first, and the children are meant to grow up inside the shelter of it, not in the place of it. Asking your spouse to come back to bed is not selfishness. It is stewardship. It is guarding the very thing your children are quietly counting on to hold together.

Because here is what the one on the empty side of the bed will almost never say out loud: it makes you feel single. Married on paper, and alone in practice. You move through a shared life all day long, and then at night you lie down by yourself like someone who never found anyone at all, and something in your chest goes hollow. That hollowness does not stay politely in the bedroom either. It follows you into your prayers. You begin to wonder where God is in a marriage that has started to feel like two tired roommates managing a household. You feel guilty for being lonely in a home that is full of people you love. You wonder if it is even right to grieve something as small as where you sleep. But it is not small. The ache you feel is the ache of a real oneness being slowly starved, and even your faith can feel thin and far away when the person you gave your whole life to has become someone you wave to in the hallway at midnight.

The way back is not complicated, though it does ask both of you to decide the empty bed is worth fighting for. It means settling the little ones and then actually rising and coming back. It means trading a little easier sleep for a marriage that does not slowly forget itself. It means praying about it together instead of resenting each other over it, and asking God to rekindle a closeness that sheer exhaustion has buried. "Though a man might prevail against one who is alone, two will withstand him. A threefold cord is not quickly broken" (Ecclesiastes 4:12). Your marriage was meant to be that cord, with God himself as the strand woven through the two of you, holding fast even on the tired nights.

So, have you reached across the bed lately and found it cold and empty, and wondered if you are even still married? You are. You are still married, and the very fact that the empty space aches is proof that the oneness is still alive in you and still worth coming home to. Settle the little ones, and then come back to bed. The person on the other side has been lying awake in the dark, hoping you finally would.
