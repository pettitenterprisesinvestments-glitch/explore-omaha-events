<p>Dear parent,</p>

<p>If there is one thing I would ask you to plant in your child's heart above almost any other, it would be kindness. Not the soft, sentimental kind we put on greeting cards, but the sturdy, deliberate kindness that notices the lonely child at the edge of the playground and walks over anyway. In a world that so often rewards the loud, the clever, and the quick, raising a tender heart can feel like swimming against the current. Yet I am convinced it is one of the most important and lasting works a parent ever does, and I want to encourage you that it is within your reach, even on the ordinary days when you feel you have little left to give.</p>

<p>Kindness is not a personality trait that some children are simply born with and others lack. It is a habit of the heart, and habits are taught. A child learns compassion the same way he learns to tie his shoes or speak his first words, through patient repetition and the steady example of someone who loves him. This is good news, because it means kindness does not depend on whether your child is naturally outgoing or shy, spirited or gentle. Every child can be taught to consider others, and every parent has the standing to teach it. You do not need a degree or a perfect home. You need only the willingness to model what you hope to see.</p>

<p>And modeling is where it begins. Our children are forever watching us when we think they are not. They see how we speak to the tired cashier, how we treat the family member who is difficult to love, whether we grumble about the neighbor or carry a meal to her door. They learn far more from the kindness we live than the kindness we lecture about. If we want children who are gentle with the weak and patient with the slow, we must let them catch us being gentle and patient ourselves. The words "be kind" carry little weight when a child has just watched us snap at the person who cut us off in traffic. But when our daily conduct quietly matches our instruction, the lesson sinks down into the bones.</p>

<p>It also helps to give kindness somewhere to go. Compassion grows muscle through use, and children need real chances to practice it. Let your child carry the groceries for an elderly neighbor, write a note to a grandparent, share a toy that costs him something to share. When you serve others as a family, you are not merely doing a good deed; you are showing your child that his hands were made to lift others up. The Scriptures remind us that we are to love our neighbor as ourselves, and a child begins to understand that ancient command not by hearing it explained but by living it out in small, concrete acts that become second nature over time.</p>

<p>I would also gently encourage you to teach your child that kindness includes those who can offer nothing in return. It is easy to be generous with friends and those we hope to impress. The deeper work is to notice the overlooked, to befriend the child no one else will sit with, to speak gently to the person the world has decided does not matter. When you point your child toward the forgotten and the struggling, you are teaching him that every person carries dignity and worth. This is the kindness that changes the world, and it begins at your kitchen table long before it ever reaches the schoolyard.</p>

<p>Be patient, too, with the slow progress. Children are still children, and they will be selfish one hour and saintly the next. A kind child is not a finished child but a growing one, and your job is not to demand perfection but to keep gently turning his face toward others. When he fails, and he will, do not shame him. Show him the better way, forgive him as you hope to be forgiven, and try again tomorrow. The goal is not a child who performs kindness for praise but one whose heart has been slowly shaped to care.</p>

<p>So take heart. The quiet investments you are making, the apologies you model, the compassion you show when no one is keeping score, are not wasted. You are raising a person who will one day carry comfort into rooms you will never enter, who will be gentle with people you will never meet. Few labors matter more, and few are more worthy of your weary, faithful effort. Keep going. The tender heart you are cultivating today will bless this world long after the lessons are forgotten and only the love remains.</p>
