Most people have heard the word "grant" and immediately assumed it was not for them.

Maybe they thought you needed a special background. Maybe they assumed the application process was too complicated. Maybe they simply never had anyone sit down and break down how the system actually works.

Coach K is that person.

She spent over a decade in banking — consumer lending, commercial lending, private lending — watching who got approved for funding and who walked out empty-handed. What she noticed changed the direction of her career: the people who were getting access to capital often did not look like her community. So she made it her mission to change that.

Her own grant journey started with $500. Not glamorous. But it proved something critical — that money was out there, it did not have to be paid back, and consistency was the key to unlocking more of it. Her largest personal grant to date: $146,000, deposited directly into her business bank account.

No credit check. No repayment required.

She shared all of it in a recent episode of the Circle of Greatness podcast with host Nehemiah Davis. Here is what every parent entrepreneur needs to understand.

---

## What a Grant Actually Is

A grant is debt-free funding.

There is no credit score requirement. There is no application fee in most cases. There is no monthly payment waiting for you on the other side. It is money that organizations — corporations, foundations, government agencies, and nonprofits — set aside specifically to invest in businesses and causes they believe in.

Chick-fil-A distributes $5 million in grants annually. They are not looking for perfect credit scores. They are looking for businesses making a measurable difference in their communities. A 10 to 15 minute application telling your story could result in $325,000 walking through your door.

That is not a rumor. That is a program currently open and accepting applicants.

The reason most entrepreneurs miss this is not because they are unqualified. It is because they are not applying.

---

## The Number That Changes Everything

Coach K shared a statistic that reframes the entire conversation around grants.

If the average entrepreneur applies for 21 to 30 grants per month, they have a 99 percent chance of receiving at least one grant.

Read that again.

Twenty to thirty applications per month. That is less than one per day. And the probability of success becomes nearly certain.

She posts five grants every Monday that her community can apply for that week. That alone covers 20 per month. She also recommends a platform called Hello Skip, which allows you to pay a monthly subscription and have it automatically apply for every grant that flows through their system — over one million dollars in grants currently listed on that platform.

The math is simple. If you apply to 240 grants in a year and close just 10 percent of them, that is 24 grants. Close two percent and you still get five. And if one of those five happens to be a $146,000 grant or a $325,000 corporate community investment — the entire year changes.

## Getting Your Foundation Right First

Coach K is direct about this: you cannot win money you are not structured to receive.

Before you apply for a single grant, these three things need to be in place.

*Your business plan.* Most entrepreneurs have a vision but never put it on paper. A business plan is not just a document — it is your grant proposal. Without it, you are asking an organization to fund a dream they cannot see. With it, your vision becomes a credible investment.

*Your business address.* This one surprises people. Using your home address on business documents is one of the most common and costly mistakes entrepreneurs make. To a bank or grant organization, a home-based business signals limited overhead needs — which caps the level of funding they consider you eligible for. Coach K's recommendation is to lease space from a friend or colleague who has a commercial building, paying them a small monthly fee to use that address. You get a credible brick-and-mortar presence. They generate a new income stream. Both win.

*Your banking relationship.* Do not walk into a bank asking for $100,000 if you have not even opened a deposit account. Banks use internal point systems — a checking account is a point, a savings account is a point, a credit card is a point. The more products you hold and actively use, the more the bank trusts you. Bring your banker coffee. Introduce yourself. Make yourself a familiar face before you ever need anything. The relationship you build today is what gets someone to fight for your loan approval six months from now.

## How to Stand Out When Everyone Is Applying

The question Nehemiah asked that most entrepreneurs are thinking: if hundreds of people are applying for the same grant, how do you separate yourself?

Coach K's answer: the wow factor.

You have roughly ten to fifteen seconds to grab a grant reviewer's attention. In that window, you need to show one thing clearly — your business is making a measurable, real impact on actual people in your community.

Grant organizations are not funding businesses that are looking for a handout. They are investing in businesses that are already solving problems and want help scaling that solution. Your application needs to reflect that. Tell the story of the people your business has helped. Show the change you are creating. Make the reviewer feel the impact before they finish reading the first paragraph.

She also recommends a strategic move that many entrepreneurs overlook: having both a for-profit and a nonprofit entity. Many grants are exclusive to nonprofits. Others are limited to for-profit companies. If you have both, you can apply either way — doubling your eligible opportunities. If starting a nonprofit is not part of your vision, Coach K suggests partnering with an established nonprofit in your space and applying jointly. The connection between your for-profit business and a nonprofit partner is exactly how she structured the $146,000 grant that changed her trajectory.

---

## Grants and Business Credit Together

While grants are the primary focus, Coach K advocates for using both tools strategically.

A line of credit is different from a loan. It sits available as a safety net — you draw from it only when you need it and pay interest only on what you use. It does not sit on your books the way a lump sum loan does. For a growing business, that flexibility matters.

She also recommends credit unions over large national banks for personal service. A smaller institution that actually understands your vision and your community is far more likely to advocate for you during the approval process than a branch where you are an account number among millions.

The goal is not to rely on one tool. It is to build a financial infrastructure — grants for growth, lines of credit for cash flow, banking relationships for long-term leverage — so your business is never fragile.

## The Bigger Picture

Coach K's student received $1.7 million in grant funding for a nonprofit that helps single mothers navigate postpartum recovery. That money did not require a perfect credit score. It required a clear mission, a solid structure, and a consistent commitment to applying.

There is money designated right now — by corporations, foundations, and government programs — for businesses doing exactly what you are doing. The only thing standing between you and access to it is whether you are in the game or watching from the sideline.

As Nehemiah put it: being selfish about not going to get that money is not humility. It is leaving resources on the table that could amplify the impact you are already making.
