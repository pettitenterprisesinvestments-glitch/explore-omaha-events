Just west of downtown Omaha sits a place that began with almost nothing and grew into one of the most inspiring stories in America. It started in 1917, when a young Irish-born Catholic priest named Father Edward J. Flanagan looked at the homeless and forgotten boys of the city, the runaways and the orphans and the ones everyone else had given up on, and refused to believe they were beyond hope. With a borrowed house and a few dollars he could scarcely spare, he opened a home for them, and he built it on a single radical idea that would echo for a century: that there is no such thing as a bad boy, only a child who has not yet been loved and guided well.

Father Flanagan's home grew quickly, far faster than his tiny budget could comfortably hold. In 1921, he moved his expanding family of boys to Overlook Farm on the western edge of Omaha, and there something remarkable took shape. It was not merely an orphanage. Flanagan built a real community, a self-governing village where the boys had their own elected leaders, their own schools, their own chapel, and a sense of belonging that most had never known. The boys learned trades, played sports, worshiped, and discovered that someone believed in them. The place became known simply as Boys Town.

For years it was Omaha's quiet miracle, but in 1938 the whole world learned about it. Hollywood released a film called Boys Town, with Spencer Tracy playing Father Flanagan in a performance so moving it won him an Academy Award. Suddenly Flanagan's little Nebraska village was famous everywhere, and donations and admiration poured in from across the globe. Out of that fame came one of the most beloved images in American culture: the statue of an older boy carrying a younger one on his back, beneath the words, He ain't heavy, Father, he's my brother. It captured everything Flanagan believed in a single picture.

What is most remarkable is that Boys Town never became a museum piece. It is still very much alive and still doing the work Father Flanagan began. Today it cares for children across the country, and it is home to the renowned Boys Town National Research Hospital, a leader in helping kids with serious medical needs. A simple idea born in an Omaha rented house in 1917 grew into a national force for good that has touched the lives of countless children.

And here is the wonderful part for anyone planning a trip to Omaha: you can visit. The historic village of Boys Town is open to the public, and walking its peaceful campus is a genuinely moving experience. You can step inside the beautiful Dowd Memorial Chapel, explore the Hall of History that tells Father Flanagan's story, and see for yourself the famous statue that has comforted so many. Families especially love a quirky treasure on the campus, the World's Largest Ball of Stamps, a delightful surprise that kids never forget.

There is something about Boys Town that stays with you. In an age that can feel cynical, it stands as living proof of what one person's faith and stubborn love can build, and of the truth that every child is worth fighting for. It is history you can walk through, hope you can see with your own eyes.

So when you plan your visit to Omaha, make room for Boys Town. Bring the family, take your time, and let the story sink in.
