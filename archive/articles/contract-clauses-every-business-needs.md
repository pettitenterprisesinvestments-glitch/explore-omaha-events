Most disputes between businesses do not happen because someone was dishonest. They happen because two people remembered the same conversation differently, and there was nothing in writing to settle the question. A good contract is not a sign of distrust; it is a shared memory that everyone agreed to in advance. And the strength of that memory depends almost entirely on which clauses you bothered to include. A handful of well-chosen provisions can save you from the most common and costly fights a small business ever has.

Start with the foundation of any agreement: a clear description of the work. The scope of work, sometimes called the statement of work, spells out exactly what is being delivered, by whom, and to what standard. Vagueness here is where projects go to die. When a contract says a vendor will build a website but never defines how many pages, what features, or when it is finished, you have planted the seed of an argument. A precise scope protects both sides, because it tells everyone what counts as done, and just as importantly, what would be an extra you can charge for.

Next comes the clause owners care about most once money is tight: payment terms. A strong agreement does not just name a price; it says when payment is due, in what amounts, by what method, and what happens if it is late. Will you require a deposit before starting? Will you bill in milestones? Do unpaid invoices accrue a late fee after a set number of days? Spelling this out does more than secure your cash flow. It quietly signals that you are a professional who expects to be paid on time, and it gives you something concrete to point to when a client drifts past the due date.

The third essential is the one people least like to think about: how the relationship ends. A termination clause explains how either party can walk away, how much notice is required, and what is owed when they do. Without it, you can find yourself trapped in a deal that no longer makes sense, or blindsided when the other side vanishes. A good termination clause is like knowing where the exits are before the movie starts; you hope you never need them, but you are grateful they are clearly marked.

Closely related is a clause that decides what happens when there is a genuine disagreement. A dispute resolution provision can require the parties to attempt mediation or to use arbitration before anyone races to court, and it can specify which state's laws govern the agreement and where any dispute will be heard. These details feel like fine print until the day they become the whole ballgame. Deciding the rules of engagement while everyone is calm is far cheaper than fighting about the rules themselves later.

Beyond these, a few more provisions earn their place in most agreements. A confidentiality clause protects sensitive information you share while working together. A clause clarifying ownership of work product decides who owns what gets created, which matters enormously for anything involving designs, code, or content. A limitation of liability clause caps how much you could owe if something goes wrong, keeping a small mistake from turning into a business-ending judgment. And a force majeure clause addresses what happens when events truly outside anyone's control disrupt the deal.

The theme running through all of these is simple: a contract earns its keep in the hard moments, not the easy ones. When everyone is happy and the work is going well, the document sits in a drawer. It only matters when something breaks, and at that point you cannot add the clause you wish you had written. That is why the time to think carefully about your agreements is before you sign them, and ideally with a professional who has seen how these disputes actually unfold.
