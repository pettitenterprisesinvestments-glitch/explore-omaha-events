<p>Dear fellow parent,</p>

<p>If you have ever stood in your own living room and felt as though you were competing with a glowing rectangle for your child's attention, I want you to know that you are not failing, and you are not alone. The pull of the screen is real, and it is engineered to be strong. But there is a quieter, older way of being a family, and it is still available to you. It begins not with a dramatic vow to throw every device into the river, but with a simple decision to look up more often than you look down.</p>

<p>I am writing to encourage you toward a screen-free home, or at least a screen-fewer one, not because screens are uniquely wicked, but because the hours of childhood are few and they do not return. The years in which a child wants to climb into your lap, tell you about a beetle on the sidewalk, or ask you the same question four times in a row are brief. They pass whether we are present for them or not. A screen will never resent being set aside, but a child will quietly learn whether he ranks above or below the device in your hand. That lesson is taught in small moments, not grand ones, and it is taught more by what we do than by what we say.</p>

<p>The first gift of a less-connected home is attention, and attention is a form of love. When you close the laptop and sit on the floor, when you answer the question without glancing away, you are telling your child something true and steadying: you matter more to me than anything happening somewhere else. Children read our eyes long before they understand our words. A child who is regularly looked at, listened to, and laughed with grows up with a deep, wordless confidence that he is worth someone's full presence. No app can deliver that, and no amount of educational content can replace it.</p>

<p>The second gift is the recovery of boredom, which our age has wrongly treated as an enemy. A bored child, left without a screen to fill the silence, will eventually do the most important work of childhood. He will invent a game, build a fort, draw a crooked map, pester a sibling into a truce, or wander outside to see what the ants are doing. Boredom is the soil in which imagination grows. When we rush to fill every empty minute with a video, we are pulling up the seedling to check whether it has roots. Trust the silence. Let your children be a little bored, and watch what they make of it.</p>

<p>The third gift is the dinner table, and here I would gently press you. Of all the screen-free spaces a family can protect, the shared meal is the most powerful and the most easily lost. A table where faces are turned toward one another, where the day is recounted and grace is spoken, becomes the steady heartbeat of a household. It is where children learn to listen, to wait their turn, to laugh at a parent's tired joke, and to belong. Guard that table. It does not require fine food or clever conversation. It only requires that the screens stay off and the people stay present.</p>

<p>None of this means we must live in fear or pretend the modern world away. Screens have their place, and a family can use them wisely without being ruled by them. The aim is not perfection but proportion. You do not need to win every battle over every minute. You need only to keep tipping the balance, again and again, toward the people in the room and away from the people on the screen. Children are forgiving of our stumbles when they can see the direction of our hearts.</p>

<p>So set down the phone a little earlier tonight. Let the evening be slower than the world says it should be. Read the bedtime story even when you are tired, and read it badly, and read it anyway. These ordinary acts are not small. They are the very substance of a childhood your child will carry for the rest of his life, and they are the quiet, faithful work to which you have been called.</p>

<p>You are doing more good than you know. Keep looking up.</p>
