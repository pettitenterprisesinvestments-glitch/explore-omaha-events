Have you and your spouse ever caught yourselves on opposite sides of the same child, one of you saying yes while the other says no, and felt the ground of your marriage shift a little beneath your feet?

Every couple who raises children eventually discovers that parenting is one of the great tests of a marriage. It is rarely the diapers or the sleepless nights that strain you most. It is the hundred small moments when you realize the two of you do not instinctively agree on how to raise the people you love most. One of you is firmer; the other is gentler. One leans toward grace, the other toward consequences. And a child, even a very small one, learns with remarkable speed exactly where the seam between the two of you lies, and how to wedge a little hand into it.

Here is the first lie this season loves to tell: that the goal is to be right. When you and your spouse disagree over bedtime or screen time or how to handle the tantrum in the grocery aisle, it is easy to believe the most important thing is to win the point, to prove that your way is the wiser one. But your children do not most need the correct parent to prevail. They need to know that the two people holding their world together are holding it together. Security is built far more by unity than by getting every parenting decision exactly right. "If a house is divided against itself, that house will not be able to stand" (Mark 3:25), and small children are expert at finding the dividing line.

Which leads to a simple but costly discipline: disagree in private, present a united front in public. It is a quiet betrayal to overrule your spouse in front of the children, to roll your eyes at their decision or whisper to a child that mom or dad is being too strict. Whatever momentary comfort it buys you, it teaches your children that your authority can be split, and a divided authority is one they will spend years learning to exploit. Back each other in the moment, even when you would have chosen differently, and save the real conversation for when little ears are asleep.

Here is the second lie, the one that frightens good couples: that if you do not see eye to eye on parenting, something must be wrong with your marriage. But two people who came from two different families, raised in two different ways, will of course bring two different instincts to the hardest job they will ever share. Disagreement is not the problem. The only real question is whether you process those disagreements as teammates solving a problem together, or as opponents each trying to win a child to their side. The same marriage that can be torn by parenting can also be knit closer by it, if you let each hard decision drive you toward each other instead of apart.

So build the habits of a team. Talk about the big things before they happen, so you are not improvising your values in the heat of a meltdown. Divide the labor honestly, and notice out loud the load your spouse is carrying. And above all, pray together over your children, because nothing unites two parents like kneeling beside the same crib and asking God for the same child. "Train up a child in the way he should go" (Proverbs 22:6) speaks of a single way, one path you walk together, not two competing roads.

So the next time you find yourselves on opposite sides of the same child, stop, and remember that you were never meant to be on opposite sides at all. Step back, take your spouse's hand, and get on the same side of the table. Your children do not need a winner. They need a team. And the home where mom and dad parent as one is the safest place a child will ever know.
