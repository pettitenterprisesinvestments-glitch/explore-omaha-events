Dear Mom and Dad,

If you have ever stood in a toy aisle and watched a child's eyes move from one bright box to the next, never quite landing because there is always something newer just past it, then you already understand the quiet battle you are fighting. We are raising children in a world that is very good at teaching them to want and very poor at teaching them to be thankful. The want comes naturally. The thankfulness has to be planted, watered, and tended like anything else worth growing. I want to encourage you today that this work, though slow and often invisible, may be one of the most important things you ever give your children.

Gratitude is not a manners drill. It is tempting to think we have done our job when a child mumbles "thank you" on cue, but a polite reflex and a grateful heart are not the same thing. One can be trained in an afternoon. The other takes years. Real gratitude is a posture of the soul that says, "I did not earn all of this, and I am glad for it." Our task is not merely to produce children who say the right words, but children who actually feel the weight and wonder of what they have been given. That kind of heart is caught more than it is taught, which means the first and most important thing you can do is let your children watch you be thankful.

Children become grateful by watching grateful parents. They are always studying us, far more than they are listening to us. When you pause before a meal to give thanks, when you speak of your home and your work and your family as gifts rather than burdens, when you resist the urge to complain about what you lack and instead name what you have, your children are learning a language. They learn that a full table is not a given, that a warm bed is a kindness, that the people around them are treasures and not fixtures. You do not have to lecture them. You simply have to let them see that your own heart is full.

Gratitude also grows in homes where blessings are spoken out loud. There is something powerful about naming good things by name. A family that regularly stops to say what it is thankful for, whether around the dinner table or at the end of a long day, is training its children to look for the good and to acknowledge its source. Children who grow up in such homes develop a habit of noticing. They begin to see the ordinary mercies that the rest of the world rushes past, and a child who notices is well on the way to a child who is thankful.

It helps, too, to let your children wait and work for the things they want. A child who receives everything the moment he asks for it will struggle to be grateful, not because he is bad, but because he has never felt the gap between longing and having. That gap is where gratitude is born. When a child saves for something, waits for it, and finally holds it in his hands, he understands its value in a way that no instant gift can teach. Do not be afraid to let your children want something for a while. You are not depriving them. You are giving them the gift of appreciation.

And finally, teach your children that gratitude has a direction. Thankfulness that floats in the air with no one to receive it eventually fades. The deepest gratitude is the kind that looks upward and says thank you to the God who gives every good gift, and outward to the people who love and provide and sacrifice. When children learn that the right response to blessing is to bless others in return, gratitude stops being a feeling and becomes a way of living. They begin to share more freely, to serve more gladly, and to carry a quiet contentment that the world cannot give and cannot take away.

So take heart, weary parent. You will not see the fruit of this work tomorrow. You will plant seeds in soil you cannot inspect, and you will wonder, on the hard days, whether anything is taking root at all. But one day you will catch your grown child thanking someone with a sincerity that cannot be faked, or pausing to count blessings without being asked, and you will know. The thankfulness you modeled when they were small has become their own. Keep planting. Keep giving thanks where they can see you. A grateful child is one of the great quiet victories of a faithful home, and it is worth every patient day it takes to raise one.

With you in the work,
