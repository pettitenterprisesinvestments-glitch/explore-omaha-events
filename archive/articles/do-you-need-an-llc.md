If you have started a business or are thinking about it, you have almost certainly heard the same three letters repeated like a mantra: LLC. Form an LLC, everyone says, as if it were a magic shield you buy once and never think about again. But the real question is not whether LLCs are good. It is whether one is right for you, what it actually does, and what it does not. Understanding that is the difference between protection you can count on and a false sense of security.

Start with the structure most people already have without realizing it: the sole proprietorship. The moment you start doing business on your own and do nothing else, you are a sole proprietor by default. It is simple, cheap, and requires almost no paperwork, which is exactly why so many people begin there. But it carries one enormous catch. In a sole proprietorship, there is no legal separation between you and your business. If the business is sued or runs up debts it cannot pay, your personal assets, your savings, your car, even your home, can be on the line. The same is true of a general partnership, which is essentially a sole proprietorship with more than one owner, and which can also make you responsible for what your partner does.

This is the gap the limited liability company is designed to close. An LLC creates a separate legal entity that stands between your business and your personal life. If the company is sued or cannot pay its debts, generally only the business's assets are exposed, and your personal property stays protected. That single feature, the liability shield, is the main reason the LLC has become the default choice for small business owners. It also offers flexibility in how you are taxed and far less formality than a corporation, making it a comfortable middle ground for most.

Then there is the corporation, and a tax election you have probably heard mentioned in the same breath: the S corporation. A corporation is a more formal, more structured entity, traditionally favored by businesses that plan to bring on investors or eventually go public. The S corporation is not a separate kind of entity so much as a tax status that an LLC or corporation can elect, one that can, in the right circumstances, reduce self-employment taxes for owners who pay themselves a salary. These options can be powerful, but they come with more rules, more paperwork, and more reasons to have professional guidance before you choose.

So how do you decide? The honest answer is that it depends on your risk, your income, and your plans. If your business has almost no chance of being sued and earns very little, a sole proprietorship may be perfectly reasonable to start. But the moment you have real liability exposure, employees, valuable assets, meaningful income, or partners, the protection of an LLC usually becomes well worth the modest cost and effort. And as your profits grow, a conversation about whether an S corporation election makes sense can save you real money. The key is to match the structure to your actual situation rather than copying whatever a friend did.

It is also worth saying clearly what an LLC does not do. It does not protect you if you personally guarantee a loan, if you commit fraud, or if you fail to treat the business as genuinely separate by mixing personal and business funds. The shield only holds if you respect it. Forming the entity is the first step; running it properly is what keeps the protection alive.

Choosing a business structure is one of those decisions that looks like simple paperwork but quietly shapes your taxes, your liability, and your future for years.
