<p>Dear parent, I want to tell you something you may have forgotten in the noise of an ordinary day: the words you speak over your child do not vanish when the conversation ends. They settle. They take root in a place deeper than memory, and long after your son or daughter has grown and gone, your voice will still be speaking. The tone you used at the breakfast table, the name you called them when you were proud, the sentence you repeated when you were tired &mdash; these become the quiet inner voice your child carries into every room they ever enter. This is one of the most sacred and underestimated parts of raising a family, and it costs you nothing but attention.</p>

<p>Children are listening even when they appear not to be. A small child absorbs your words the way dry ground absorbs rain, without resistance and without filter. When you say &ldquo;you are a hard worker&rdquo; or &ldquo;you have a kind heart,&rdquo; you are not merely complimenting a moment; you are handing your child a description of who they are. They will believe you, because you are the first and most trusted authority they know. This is a staggering responsibility, but it is also a gift. You get to be the one who tells them the truth about their worth before the world ever gets the chance to tell them something smaller.</p>

<p>Consider how much of our speech as parents drifts toward correction. We are quick to name the spilled milk, the unfinished chore, the sharp tone with a sibling, and slow to name the patience, the courage, the gentleness we also see. Correction has its place, and a loving parent does not flinch from it. But a home where the loudest words are always about what went wrong slowly teaches a child to listen for failure. The blessing of an encouraging parent is not flattery and not the empty praise that calls everything wonderful. It is the discipline of noticing what is genuinely good and saying it out loud. &ldquo;I saw how you shared with your brother.&rdquo; &ldquo;You kept trying when it was hard.&rdquo; &ldquo;I am glad God gave you to us.&rdquo; These are small sentences that build a large soul.</p>

<p>There is an old and beautiful practice in the life of faith of speaking a blessing over a child &mdash; of a parent laying a hand on a young head and naming a future full of hope. You do not need a formal ceremony to do this. You can do it in the car, at bedtime, on the walk to school. To bless a child is simply to speak life over them on purpose: to tell them they are loved, that they belong, that they were made for a reason and that you believe in who they are becoming. A child who hears this regularly grows up with a kind of inner stability that no trophy or test score can give. They know whose they are, and that knowledge steadies them when the world grows unkind.</p>

<p>I want to be honest with you about the harder side of this truth, because you deserve honesty more than comfort. The same power that lets your words build your child up means your words can also wound in ways you may never see. A careless label spoken in frustration &mdash; lazy, difficult, too much &mdash; can lodge in a child&rsquo;s heart and play on repeat for decades. If you have spoken words you regret, and every parent has, take heart: the remedy is near. Children are remarkably willing to forgive a parent who comes to them humbly and says, &ldquo;I was wrong to speak to you that way, and I am sorry.&rdquo; That apology is itself one of the most powerful words a child can ever hear, because it teaches them that love and humility belong together.</p>

<p>So speak well over your children, and do it deliberately. Choose a few true and good things and say them again and again until they become the soundtrack your child cannot unhear. Tell them they are loved before they have done anything to earn it. Praise the character you want to grow, not merely the performance you happened to see. Bless their future out loud, even when the present feels chaotic. You are not raising a child for this afternoon only; you are raising a man or a woman who will one day repeat your words to children of their own.</p>

<p>You will not get every sentence right, and you do not have to. What your child needs is not a perfect parent but a present one whose voice, on the whole, sounds like belief in them. Years from now, when they face a closed door or a long night, the voice that rises up in them to say <em>keep going, you are loved, you can do this</em> &mdash; that voice will sound a great deal like yours. Make it a voice worth carrying.</p>
