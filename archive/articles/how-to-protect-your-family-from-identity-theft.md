Identity theft is not something that only happens to other people. In 2024, the Federal Trade Commission received over 1.2 million reports of identity theft, and that number is climbing every year. Thieves do not need to steal your wallet anymore. They need your name, your Social Security number, and a few details they can find online, and suddenly they are opening credit cards, filing tax returns, or even taking out loans in your name. For a family, the damage can be devastating, and undoing it can take months.

The good news is that most identity theft is preventable if you know what to watch for and take a few simple steps ahead of time.

Understand How Thieves Get Your Information

Before you can protect yourself, it helps to know how the theft actually happens. The most common methods are data breaches, phishing emails, stolen mail, and lost or stolen wallets. A data breach at a company you do business with can expose your information without you doing anything wrong. A phishing email tricks you into clicking a link and entering your password on a fake website. Stolen mail can give a thief access to bank statements, credit card offers, and tax documents.

The key is to be aware that your information is out there, and to take steps to make it harder for someone to use it even if they get it.

Freeze Your Credit

This is the single most effective thing you can do to protect your family from identity theft. A credit freeze restricts access to your credit report, which means that even if a thief has your Social Security number, they cannot open new accounts in your name because lenders cannot check your credit. You can freeze and unfreeze your credit for free at all three major credit bureaus: Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion. It takes about 15 minutes to set up, and it is one of the strongest defenses available.

If you need to apply for credit, you can temporarily lift the freeze. When you are done, you put it back. There is no downside to having your credit frozen when you are not actively applying for new accounts.

Protect Your Children's Identities

Here is something many parents do not think about: children are prime targets for identity theft because their Social Security numbers are clean and unused. A thief who gets a child's Social Security number can open accounts and run up debt for years before anyone notices, because no one checks a child's credit report. If your child starts receiving credit card offers in the mail, or if you get a notice from the IRS about income your child supposedly earned, that is a red flag. You can freeze your child's credit at all three bureaus as well.

Be Smart About Passwords and Online Accounts

Use a different password for every important account, and use a password manager to keep track of them. Enable two-factor authentication on your email, bank, and credit accounts. Be cautious about what you share on social media, especially details like your full birth date, your address, or your mother's maiden name, all of which are commonly used as security questions.

Watch Your Mail and Your Statements

Shred any documents with personal information before you throw them away. Pick up your mail promptly, and if you are going to be out of town, have someone collect it or put a hold on delivery. Review your bank and credit card statements every month for charges you do not recognize. A small unauthorized charge is often a test before a larger theft.

Know What to Do If It Happens

If you discover that your identity has been stolen, act fast. Contact the fraud departments at any affected financial institutions. Place a fraud alert on your credit reports at all three bureaus. File a report with the Federal Trade Commission at IdentityTheft.gov, and file a report with your local police department. The FTC report will serve as proof that you are a victim, which you will need when disputing fraudulent charges.
