<p>Dear parent, if you have ever lain awake worrying that you are not giving your children enough, I want to begin by telling you something you may have stopped believing: the thing your child needs most from you is not something you have to buy, schedule, or perfect. It is you. Your attention, your steadiness, your willingness to be present in the ordinary hours — these are the riches that shape a young life. We live in a season of human history that constantly whispers that more is the answer. More activities, more lessons, more gadgets, more of everything money can reach. Yet children have not changed. What they hunger for is what children have always hungered for: to be known, to be wanted, and to belong to someone who will not leave.</p>

<p>Consider how a small child measures love. He does not count the dollars spent on his birthday; he counts whether you looked up when he showed you the drawing. She does not remember the brand of her shoes; she remembers the night you sat on the edge of her bed and listened to the long, winding story of her day. The currency of childhood is presence. When we mistake provision for presence, we can work ourselves to exhaustion buying things our children would gladly trade for one unhurried afternoon. None of this means that providing for a family is unimportant. Honest work that puts food on the table and a roof overhead is a noble and holy thing. But provision is the soil, not the harvest. The harvest is a child who knows, deep in his bones, that he is loved.</p>

<p>There is a second gift your presence gives, and it is the gift of being formed by your character. Children are not raised chiefly by our speeches; they are raised by watching who we are when we think no one is grading us. They learn patience from a parent who takes a breath instead of snapping. They learn honesty from a parent who admits a mistake at the dinner table. They learn what faithfulness looks like by watching a mother and father keep their promises to each other and to them. This is humbling, because it means our children are always learning, even in our weakest moments. But it is also freeing, because it means you do not need to be a flawless parent to be a faithful one. You only need to keep showing up, keep turning back toward what is good, and let them see you do it.</p>

<p>I want to encourage you, too, about the days that feel small and repetitive. The folding of laundry, the wiping of a counter, the thousandth reminder to brush teeth — these are not the interruptions to the real work of parenting. They are the real work. The everyday faithfulness of caring for a household is where children absorb the rhythm of a life that is ordered by love rather than by impulse. A family table where people gather and give thanks, a home where bedtime comes with a blessing instead of a screen, a Sunday set aside for worship and rest — these humble structures are not old-fashioned constraints. They are the trellis on which a young soul climbs toward maturity.</p>

<p>If you are weary, take heart. You are not behind, and you are not failing because you cannot give your children everything the world insists they must have. The Scriptures remind us that children are a heritage and a reward, not a project to be optimized. The best thing you can do tonight is not found in a store or an app. It is to put down what can wait, look your child in the eye, and let him know that of all the places you could be, you have chosen to be here, with him. That choice, made again and again across the unremarkable years, is how a child comes to understand that he is treasured by you — and, in time, that he is treasured by the God who gave him to you.</p>

<p>So do not measure your parenting by the abundance you can purchase. Measure it by the presence you can offer. Give your children your face, your patience, your honest example, and your faithful, repeated returns to the dinner table and the bedside. They will not remember most of what you bought. They will remember that you were there. And one day, when they are grown and raising children of their own, they will reach for the same simple riches — and they will know exactly where they learned them.</p>
