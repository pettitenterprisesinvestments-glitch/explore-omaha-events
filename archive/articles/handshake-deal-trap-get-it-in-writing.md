There is something deeply appealing about a handshake deal. It feels honorable and human, a sign that two people trust each other enough to skip the lawyers and the paperwork. For generations of business owners it has been a point of pride. And yet a striking number of the worst, most relationship-ending disputes a small business ever faces begin with exactly that handshake. The problem is not that the people were dishonest. The problem is that memory is unreliable, circumstances change, and a deal that lived only in two heads has no way to settle the question when those heads remember it differently.

Consider how an honest disagreement is born. Two people shake hands on a project. One walks away believing the price covered everything; the other believing it covered only the first phase. Both are sincere. Both genuinely remember the conversation their own way. Months later, when the work expands and the bill grows, there is no document to consult, only two confident and conflicting memories. What started as a friendly arrangement curdles into resentment, not because anyone lied, but because nothing was written down to anchor the truth. A written agreement is simply that anchor.

It helps to understand that a verbal agreement is often a real, legally binding contract. The myth that a deal does not count unless it is on paper is just that, a myth, in many situations. The trouble is not that oral agreements lack force; it is that they lack proof. If a verbal deal falls apart and ends up in front of a judge, the entire case can come down to your word against theirs, with little evidence to tip the balance. You may be completely in the right and still lose, simply because you cannot prove what was agreed. Writing it down does not create the obligation so much as preserve the evidence of it.

There is also a category of agreements that generally must be in writing to be enforceable at all. While the details vary, the law in most places requires written contracts for certain things, such as deals that cannot be completed within a year, agreements involving the sale of real estate, and promises to cover someone else's debt. An owner who relies on a handshake for one of these can discover too late that the agreement is not enforceable in the way they assumed. This is one more reason that, when something important is on the line, a written record is not optional but essential.

Putting a deal in writing does not require a thick contract drafted in dense legal language. Even a short, clear document, or a confirming email that lays out what was agreed, who does what, by when, and for how much, is dramatically better than nothing. The act of writing it down has a quiet secondary benefit, too: it forces both sides to discover the gaps in their understanding before the work begins, while there is still time to talk them through. Many disputes are prevented not by the document itself but by the conversation that creating it requires.

Perhaps the most important reframe is this: getting it in writing is not an act of distrust toward the other person. It is an act of respect for the relationship. The clearest, most durable partnerships are the ones where both sides know exactly what was promised, so that no honest mistake or fading memory can ever turn into a feud. Writing it down protects not just your money but the goodwill between you, which is often worth far more.
