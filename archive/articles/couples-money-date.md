For the first three years of their marriage, Kevin and Latoya had exactly one recurring fight, and it was always about money. It never started as a money fight. It started as a "why did you buy that" or a "where did our paycheck go" and then escalated into something much bigger and much older, about respect, and trust, and who got to decide.

The problem wasn't that they disagreed about money. The problem was that they only ever talked about it when something had already gone wrong. Money came up at the worst possible moments, in the heat of a surprise credit card statement, when both of them were already stressed and defensive.

A counselor gave them one piece of advice that changed everything: stop having money conversations by accident. Schedule them instead.

So they invented the money date. Once a month, on the first Sunday, they make coffee, sit down together, and look at their finances on purpose, when nothing is on fire. They review what came in, what went out, what's coming up, and what they're saving toward. The whole thing takes about forty-five minutes.

The magic isn't in the spreadsheet. It's in the timing. When you talk about money calmly, on a schedule, it stops being a series of ambushes and becomes a shared project. You're sitting on the same side of the table, looking at the numbers together, instead of facing off across them.

A few ground rules made their money dates work:

- **No blame for the past month.** You're planning forward, not prosecuting backward.
- **Both people show up informed.** Money can't be one person's secret job. The silent partner always ends up feeling either guilty or controlled.
- **Always end with something you're working toward.** A trip, a cushion, a debt you're killing off. Hope makes the discipline bearable.
- **Keep a small no-questions-asked amount for each of you.** A little personal freedom prevents a lot of resentment.

Latoya says the biggest change wasn't financial, it was emotional. The monthly date drained the fear out of the topic. They stopped flinching when the other person mentioned a purchase, because they knew there was a dedicated time and place to sort it all out.

Money is one of the top things couples fight about, but it's rarely really about the money. It's about feeling safe, feeling heard, and feeling like a team. A regular money date won't make you rich. But it can turn your most reliable argument into one of your most productive conversations.

Pick a date. Make the coffee. Sit on the same side of the table. Your future selves will thank you.
