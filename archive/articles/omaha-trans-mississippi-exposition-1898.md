For five remarkable months in 1898, the prairie city of Omaha transformed itself into one of the most beautiful places in America. From June 1 to November 1, the Trans-Mississippi and International Exposition rose from the open land of North Omaha as a dazzling temporary metropolis, and for a generation of Nebraskans it became the defining event of their lives. The fair was conceived in late 1895 by a small committee of Omaha businessmen led by the banker Gurdon Wattles, who believed the region needed a grand stage to show the nation what the American West had become. What they built exceeded even their own ambitions, drawing more than 2.6 million visitors and announcing to the country that Omaha had arrived.

The exposition occupied roughly 184 acres in the Kountze Place neighborhood, on ground that today includes Kountze Park near Twentieth and Pinkney streets. Land for the venture was made possible in part by the Omaha banker Herman Kountze, and the city that took shape there was unlike anything Nebraskans had seen. At its heart lay the Grand Court, a long formal basin of water stretching some 2,000 feet, encircled by about twenty-one stately buildings finished in brilliant white. Their classical domes, columns, and arches gleamed so brightly that the assembly earned the affectionate nickname the "New White City," an echo of the famous Chicago World's Fair of 1893. The palaces were never meant to last; they were built of temporary materials over wood frames. Yet by day they shone against the Nebraska sky, and by night thousands of electric lights traced their outlines and reflected in the still water of the lagoon, a spectacle of modern marvel to people who in many cases had grown up by lamplight.

The fair's purpose was to celebrate the development of the entire territory west of the Mississippi River, all the way to the Pacific. Inside the great halls, visitors wandered among more than four thousand exhibits showcasing agriculture, mining, manufacturing, fine art, and the newest machines of the age. Families arrived by train from across the plains, strolling the Grand Court, riding the attractions, and marveling at displays gathered from across the country and beyond. It was an education, an entertainment, and a point of civic pride all at once, the kind of communal wonder that a growing city like the one chronicled in <a href="/article/how-omaha-began">how Omaha began</a> could scarcely have imagined a few decades earlier.

Two moments lifted the exposition onto the national stage. The first was the Indian Congress, which formally opened on August 4 and became the largest gathering of Native nations of its era. More than five hundred Native Americans representing dozens of tribes and nations came to Omaha, among them many members of the Omaha and Winnebago peoples. Conceived as an ethnological exhibition, the Congress reflected the attitudes of its time, yet it also produced an extraordinary visual record. The official photographer, Frank A. Rinehart, made a celebrated series of portraits that survive today as a dignified and historically priceless gallery of faces and names from across Native America.

The second great moment came on October 12, remembered as President's Day, when President William McKinley arrived to address the crowds. His visit drew nearly one hundred thousand people to the grounds in a single day, and the sitting orator and statesman William Jennings Bryan was also among the dignitaries who came at Wattles's invitation. For a city barely two generations removed from its frontier beginnings, hosting a president before so vast an audience was an unforgettable validation.

When the gates closed on November 1, the white palaces were dismantled, and the lagoon and lawns gave way once more to ordinary city blocks. But the exposition left something that could not be torn down. It had proven that Omaha could organize, finance, and execute an event of national scale, and it filled the city with a lasting confidence that shaped its growth for decades to come. The fair's spirit of ambition and welcome can still be felt in the institutions and neighborhoods that visitors love today, from the historic <a href="/article/omaha-old-market">Old Market</a> to the city's grand civic landmarks. The New White City vanished in a single season, yet the pride it kindled became a permanent part of who Omaha is.
