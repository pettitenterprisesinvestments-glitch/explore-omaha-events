## The Secret to a Great Family Weekend

Here's something nobody tells you when you become a parent:

Your kids don't remember the expensive stuff. They remember the *moments*.

They remember the time you let them stay up late to catch fireflies in the backyard. The random Saturday you said, "Let's go explore somewhere we've never been." The messy kitchen after you made homemade pizza together and nobody cared about the flour on the floor.

That's the secret. And the best part? It works in *any* city, any season, any budget.

Here's how to build a weekend your family will actually love — no spreadsheet required.

---

## Start With One Rule: Put Phones Away for Two Hours

Before you plan a single thing, make a deal with yourself. Two hours. No scrolling, no checking email, no "just one second" moments.

Kids notice when you're *actually* there. And when you are, something shifts. They open up more. They get sillier. They stop asking for the iPad because you're more interesting than the iPad.

Two hours of real attention is worth more than a whole weekend of being physically present but mentally somewhere else.

---

## The Creative Element That Changes Everything

Here's a simple idea that works for kids from age 3 to 13:

**Give them a camera — or your old phone — and a mission.**

Before you head out to a park, a festival, a farmers market, or even just a walk around the neighborhood, tell them: *"Your job today is to find five interesting things to photograph."*

Watch what happens.

Suddenly they're *looking*. Really looking. At the way light hits a puddle. At a funny-shaped leaf. At an old man feeding pigeons. At their little brother's shoelace coming untied.

At the end of the day, sit together and look at what they captured. Ask them why they chose each one. You will be genuinely surprised by what goes on inside their heads.

This one little activity turns an ordinary outing into an adventure — and it sparks conversations you never would have had otherwise.

---

## 5 Weekend Moves That Work in Any City

These aren't Omaha-specific. They work whether you live downtown, in the suburbs, or somewhere in between.

### 1. Find the Free Thing First

Every city has free stuff happening on weekends — outdoor concerts, library events, farmers markets, nature trails, community festivals. Start there. Free events have a different energy. People are relaxed. Kids can just *be* without the pressure of "we paid for this, so be having fun."

### 2. Let One Kid Be the Planner

Take turns letting each child plan Saturday morning. Give them a simple budget (even if it's "free things only") and let them pick. You might end up feeding ducks at a pond you'd never noticed, or at a playground that's actually pretty awesome once you get there.

This builds decision-making skills. It builds confidence. And it means *they* can't complain — because they chose it.

### 3. Do Something with Your Hands

Cooking together. Building something. Painting rocks. Making a birdhouse. Any activity where you create something tangible gives kids a deep sense of pride. "We *made* that." Hang it on the wall. Put it in the garden. Let it be imperfect and real.

### 4. Go Somewhere You've Never Been

In your own city, right now, there are probably five places you've driven past a hundred times and never stopped. A trail. A historic building. A small bakery. A neighborhood you've never walked through.

Make it a habit: once a month, explore something new within 20 miles of home. You'll start seeing your city through fresh eyes — and your kids will grow up knowing that *curious people are never bored*.

### 5. End the Weekend with a Family Question

At dinner on Sunday, go around the table and ask: *"What was your favorite five minutes of this weekend?"*

Not the best part. The best *five minutes*. It's specific enough that kids actually think about it. And the answers will surprise you every time. Half the time, their favorite five minutes was something you barely noticed — a silly dance in the car, a conversation at the breakfast table, a moment that cost nothing.

That's the reminder you need every single week.

---

## The Bigger Picture

Here's what all of this adds up to:

You are not just planning weekends. You are building your family's *culture* — the stories you tell, the traditions you keep, the way your kids learn to experience the world.

A kid who grows up going to farmers markets and exploring parks and making things with their hands? They become adults who know how to find joy in simple places. Adults who don't need to spend a lot of money to feel rich in life.

That's a gift that lasts longer than any toy or theme park ticket.
