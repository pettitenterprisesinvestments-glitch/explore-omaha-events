You know how you tell yourself you have this one handled? Years ago, maybe after your first child was born or when you bought your house, you sat down and made a will. You felt responsible, grown-up, prepared. And ever since, whenever the subject of estate planning comes up, a quiet voice in your head says, I already did that. I'm covered.

That quiet voice is comforting. It is also, for a great many people, dangerously wrong.

Here is the false belief worth examining: that a will is a one-time task, a box you check once and never think about again. It feels true because the document still exists, sitting in a drawer or a file somewhere, signed and official. But a will is not a monument. It is a snapshot of your life and your wishes at one single moment, and the cruel truth is that your life almost certainly did not stand still after you signed it.

Think about everything that has changed since the day you wrote it. Maybe you have had more children, or your children have grown up. Maybe you have married, divorced, or remarried. Maybe the guardian you named for your kids has moved away, fallen out of your life, or is no longer someone you would trust with your most precious responsibility. Maybe the person you named to carry out your wishes has passed away. Maybe you have bought property, started a business, opened new accounts, or moved to a new state with entirely different laws. An old will frozen in time can quietly contradict every one of those changes.

This is what makes an outdated will so deceptive. A person with no will at all at least knows they are unprotected. But a person clinging to a stale will believes they are safe while their document quietly points to the wrong people, names guardians who cannot serve, and divides assets that no longer exist in the way it describes. When the day comes that the will is actually needed, the gap between what it says and what your life became can spark exactly the confusion, delay, and family conflict you wrote it to prevent.

The truth is gentler and more manageable than the fear. Keeping a will current is not a huge ordeal. It simply means treating your will as a living document that gets a look whenever your life takes a significant turn. The major life events are the triggers: a marriage or divorce, a birth or death in the family, a big change in your finances, a move to another state, or the loss of a person you named to a role. When one of those happens, your will needs to catch up. And even when life feels steady, a periodic review every few years is a wise habit, because laws change even when your circumstances do not.

None of this requires you to start over from scratch. Often it is a matter of a simple update, confirming that the right people are still named, that your guardians are still willing and able, and that your wishes still match the life you are actually living. The goal is for your most important document to describe the family you have now, not the family you had a decade ago.

So close the loop on that comforting voice in your head. Yes, you made a will, and that was a genuinely responsible thing to do. But ask yourself honestly: does it still reflect your life today, or the life you had when you signed it? If you are not sure, that uncertainty is the answer, and it is worth resolving while it is easy.
