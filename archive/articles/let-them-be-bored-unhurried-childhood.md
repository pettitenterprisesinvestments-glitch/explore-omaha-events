Dear Mom and Dad,

There is a particular kind of guilt that visits parents in the quiet moments, usually right after we have told a child, once again, that we are too busy. We hear the longing in their voice, and we feel the weight of everything still undone, and somewhere in the middle of that tension we begin to believe a quiet lie: that a good childhood is a full one. We fill the calendar. We fill the silence. We fill every empty stretch of an afternoon with one more activity, one more screen, one more thing to keep little hands occupied. And all the while, we wonder why our children seem restless, why we feel hollowed out, why the days blur together without ever quite landing in the heart. I want to offer you a gentler way to think about it. The most generous gift you can give your children may not be more. It may be less.

Consider how a child actually grows. Not in the rush, but in the pause. A boy who is handed a screen the moment boredom appears never learns what to do with an empty hour, and so he never discovers the small wonders that boredom was about to hand him. A girl who is shuttled from one lesson to the next, every block of her week accounted for, rarely has the long, unhurried afternoon in which imagination wakes up and stretches. The very moments we are tempted to rescue our children from are the moments in which they become themselves. Boredom is not the enemy of a rich childhood. It is the soil in which a rich childhood grows.

This is hard to trust, because an unhurried home looks, at first, like an unproductive one. There is a child lying on the floor staring at the ceiling. There is a long stretch of quiet that you are itching to fill. But watch what happens if you hold your nerve. The staring becomes wondering. The wondering becomes a question, then a plan, then a fort built from couch cushions, then a story acted out in the backyard. Children who are given room to be bored learn to entertain themselves, to invent, to sit with their own thoughts without panic. These are not small skills. They are the foundations of a steady, creative, grateful adult. We are not raising children to be endlessly amused. We are raising them to become people who can be at peace in a quiet room.

There is something deeper here too, something the slower pace makes possible. When the noise dies down, children begin to notice. They notice the bird at the window and the way the light moves across the kitchen floor in the late afternoon. They notice you. A family that is always rushing rarely talks, not really, because real conversation needs margin, the unscheduled minutes in which a child suddenly tells you what is actually on his heart. Scripture reminds us to be still, and there is wisdom in that command for the whole household. Stillness is where attention lives, and attention is simply love with its eyes open. The unhurried home is the home where a child learns he is worth slowing down for.

None of this means a good parent does nothing. It means we become more deliberate about what we say yes to. It is good and right to work hard, to provide, to give your children opportunities you never had. But every yes is also a no, and a calendar packed with good things can quietly crowd out the best ones, the long dinners and the wandering walks and the bedtime talks that no enrichment program can replace. If you are weary of the pace, you have permission to stop chasing it. Your children do not need a fuller schedule. They need you, present and unhurried, more than they need one more thing to do. I wrote once about how <a href="https://exploreomaha.com/article/your-children-need-you-more-than-they-need-more">your children need you more than they need more</a>, and the truth of it grows clearer the slower I go.

So let them be bored. Let the afternoon stretch out with nothing planned. Resist the urge to fill every silence, and watch what your children build inside it. The childhood you are tempted to optimize is not a problem to be solved but a season to be savored, and it is shorter than it feels. One day the house will be quiet for reasons you did not choose, and you will not wish you had signed them up for one more thing. You will be grateful for every slow, ordinary, unhurried hour you spent simply being their parent. Guard those hours. They are the whole point.

With you in the work,
