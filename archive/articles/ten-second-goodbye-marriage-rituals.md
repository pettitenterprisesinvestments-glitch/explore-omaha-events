For most of their early marriage, James and Elena said goodbye the way most of us do: a distracted "bye, love you," tossed over a shoulder somewhere between the coffee and the car keys. Two people on autopilot, already half gone before they'd left.

Then Elena's mother passed away. At the funeral, an older woman, married fifty-one years, told Elena something she never forgot: "We always kissed goodbye like we meant it. You never know which one is the last one." It sounds heavy, and it is. But it changed how Elena and James left the house.

Now they have what they jokingly call the ten-second goodbye. Before either of them walks out the door, they stop, look at each other, and actually connect, a real hug, a real kiss, a moment of eye contact. Ten seconds. That's it. But it turns out ten seconds is just long enough to remember that you love someone.

There's something almost embarrassingly simple about it. We give more intentional goodbyes to dinner guests than to the person we've promised our whole life to. We let the most important relationship in our lives run on the cheapest setting: habit.

The ten-second goodbye works because of what psychologists call a "ritual of connection," a small, repeated moment that signals *you matter to me.* These rituals are the punctuation marks of a marriage. The goodbye in the morning. The hello at the end of the day. The kiss before sleep. None of them take effort, but skipped over and over, their absence adds up to a strange, low-grade loneliness.

You can build these moments anywhere:

- A real greeting when you walk in the door, before you mention the bills or the broken dishwasher.
- A hand on the shoulder when you pass each other in the kitchen.
- A text in the middle of the day that isn't about logistics, just "thinking about you."

The point isn't romance for its own sake. It's the steady drumbeat of attention that tells your partner they're still chosen, still seen, still wanted, not just managed.

James says the ten-second goodbye changed his whole day. He leaves the house feeling tethered to something instead of just rushing off into traffic. And on the hard days, the days when they've been short with each other, that ten seconds is sometimes the bridge that keeps the small stuff from hardening into distance.

So tomorrow morning, before you grab the keys, stop. Ten seconds. Look at the person you married like you actually meant the promise. You did mean it. The goodbye is just a chance to remember.
