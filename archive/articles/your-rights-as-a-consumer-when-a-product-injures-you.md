We buy products every day without thinking twice. A kitchen appliance, a child's toy, a piece of furniture, a household tool. We trust that the things we bring into our homes are safe, because there are laws and standards that are supposed to ensure they are. But sometimes, a product is not safe. Sometimes it has a design flaw, a manufacturing defect, or a missing warning, and someone gets hurt. When that happens to you or someone in your family, the law gives you rights. Understanding those rights can make the difference between bearing the burden alone and holding the responsible party accountable.

What Product Liability Means

Product liability is the area of law that holds manufacturers, distributors, and sellers responsible for injuries caused by defective products. The core idea is simple: if a company puts a product into the stream of commerce, and that product is unreasonably dangerous because of a defect, the company can be held legally responsible for the harm it causes.

You do not have to prove that the manufacturer was careless in the traditional sense. In many cases, product liability operates under a theory called strict liability, which means that if the product was defective and that defect caused your injury, the manufacturer is responsible regardless of whether they intended any harm or took reasonable care. This is different from most other areas of personal injury law, where you typically have to prove negligence.

The Three Types of Product Defects

Product liability claims generally fall into three categories, and understanding which one applies to your situation is important.

The first is a design defect. This means the product was inherently dangerous because of how it was designed, not because of a mistake in manufacturing. Every unit of the product has the same flaw. An example would be a space heater designed in a way that makes it prone to tipping over and starting fires.

The second is a manufacturing defect. This means the design was fine, but something went wrong during the manufacturing process that made this particular product dangerous. Not every unit has the problem, only the defective ones. An example would be a batch of medication that was contaminated during production.

The third is a failure to warn, also called a marketing defect. This means the product was designed and manufactured correctly, but it did not include adequate warnings or instructions about a known danger. An example would be a cleaning chemical that can produce dangerous fumes when mixed with another common household product, but the label did not warn about it.

Who Can Be Held Responsible

One of the important things to understand about product liability is that multiple parties in the chain of commerce can potentially be held responsible. The manufacturer of the product, the manufacturer of a component part, the company that assembled or installed the product, the wholesaler or distributor, and the retail store that sold the product to you can all potentially be part of a product liability claim.

This matters because sometimes the manufacturer is a large overseas company that is difficult to sue directly. In those cases, the domestic distributor or retailer may be the party you can hold accountable. The key is that the product was sold, it was defective, and the defect caused your injury.

What to Do If a Product Injures You or a Family Member

If a product causes an injury, take the following steps. First, seek medical attention immediately. Your health and the health of your family come first, and the medical records will also serve as important evidence of the injury and its cause.

Second, preserve the product and everything that came with it. Do not throw the product away, do not try to fix it, and do not return it to the store. Keep it in the exact condition it was in when the injury occurred, along with the packaging, the instructions, the receipt, and any other materials that came with it. This is critical evidence.

Third, document everything. Take photographs of the product, the injury, and the scene where it happened. Write down exactly what occurred, including the date, the time, and the sequence of events. If there were witnesses, get their names and contact information.

Fourth, report the incident. You can file a report with the Consumer Product Safety Commission at SaferProducts.gov. This creates an official record and can help prevent others from being injured by the same product.

Fifth, do not contact the manufacturer or accept any settlement offer before you have spoken with an attorney. Manufacturers and their insurance companies may try to resolve the matter quickly and quietly, offering you a small payment in exchange for signing away your rights. Do not sign anything.

The Time Limit to File a Claim

Every state has a statute of limitations for product liability claims, which is the legal deadline for filing a lawsuit. In Nebraska, the statute of limitations for personal injury claims, including product liability, is generally four years from the date of the injury. But there are exceptions, and the clock can start at different times depending on the circumstances. If you wait too long, you lose your right to pursue a claim entirely, no matter how strong your case is.
