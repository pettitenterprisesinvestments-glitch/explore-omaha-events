My wife would come home from work, sink into the kitchen chair, and start telling me about her day, the impossible coworker, the deadline that moved again, the meeting that went sideways. And I, being a helpful and loving husband, would immediately start solving.

"Have you tried talking to your manager?" "You should just ignore him." "Here's what I would do."

And every single time, the conversation would somehow get *worse.* She'd get quiet. Sometimes a little annoyed. I'd walk away baffled, thinking, *I was literally trying to help.* It took me an embarrassingly long time to understand what was happening.

One evening she finally spelled it out. "I don't need you to fix it," she said. "I just need you to *get* it."

That was the whole thing. I'd been answering a question she wasn't asking. She wasn't presenting a problem for engineering review. She was inviting me into her day, hoping I'd say, "Wow, that sounds exhausting," and mean it. She wanted a witness, not a contractor.

This is one of the most common mismatches in marriage. One partner shares a feeling; the other hears a problem and rushes to solve it. The solver thinks they're being loving, the sharer feels brushed aside, and nobody understands why a kind gesture landed so badly.

Here's what I've learned to do instead, and it has genuinely changed our evenings:

- **Lead with empathy, not solutions.** "That sounds so frustrating" before anything else. Almost always, that's the entire thing they needed.
- **Get curious.** "What happened next?" "How are you feeling about it now?" Questions say *I'm here,* answers often say *let's wrap this up.*
- **Ask before advising.** "Do you want me to just listen, or do you want my thoughts?" This one sentence has saved me a hundred missteps. Sometimes she wants the advice. But she wants to be asked.
- **Resist the silence-filling fix.** When someone vents, a pause feels uncomfortable, and we rush to fill it with answers. Let it breathe. Presence is louder than advice.

The irony is that once I stopped trying to fix everything, she actually started asking for my opinion more. When people feel truly heard, they relax, and a relaxed person is far more open to ideas. My advice became welcome precisely because I stopped forcing it.

If your well-meaning help keeps backfiring, this might be the missing piece. Your partner usually isn't bringing you a problem to solve. They're bringing you a piece of their life to share. The most useful thing you can offer isn't a solution. It's the feeling of being understood.

Try the magic question tonight: "Do you want me to listen, or do you want ideas?" Then honor the answer.
