Sooner or later, most business owners share something valuable with someone else. You bring on an employee who learns your methods, hire a contractor who sees your customer list, or talk with a potential partner about an idea you have not yet launched. In those moments, the instinct to protect what you have built is healthy. Two tools come up again and again in that conversation, the non-disclosure agreement and the non-compete agreement, and they are among the most misunderstood instruments in business. Knowing what each actually does, and what genuinely holds up, lets you protect your business without leaning on false confidence.

Start with the more straightforward and more broadly useful of the two: the non-disclosure agreement, often called an NDA or confidentiality agreement. An NDA is a promise that the information you share will be kept confidential and not used or revealed without permission. It is the right tool whenever you need to hand sensitive information to someone, whether an employee, a vendor, a freelancer, or a prospective partner, while keeping the right to control where it goes. Because it protects information rather than restricting a person's ability to earn a living, an NDA is generally far easier to enforce than a non-compete, and it belongs in the toolkit of nearly every business that has secrets worth keeping.

The non-compete agreement is a very different animal. It attempts to restrict someone, usually a former employee or a seller of a business, from competing with you for a period of time within a certain area. The appeal is obvious: you do not want to train someone only to watch them walk across the street and use everything they learned against you. But this is precisely where owners place too much faith in a signed page, because non-competes are far harder to enforce than people assume, and the rules around them have been shifting significantly. Whether a non-compete will hold up depends heavily on where you are and on the specifics of the agreement, and in a growing number of places the restrictions are limited, disfavored, or in certain contexts unenforceable altogether.

Even where non-competes are allowed, courts tend to look at them skeptically and enforce only what is reasonable. An agreement that is too broad, lasting too long, covering too wide an area, or sweeping in more than is genuinely necessary to protect the business, can be narrowed or thrown out entirely. The lesson is not that non-competes are worthless, but that an overreaching one offers a false sense of security. A narrowly tailored agreement that protects a legitimate interest stands a far better chance than an aggressive one that tries to lock a person out of their entire profession.

This is why, for many owners, the most reliable protection comes not from trying to stop people from competing but from protecting the specific things that give the business its edge. Well-drafted confidentiality agreements guard your sensitive information. Narrower agreements, such as one preventing a former employee from soliciting your existing customers or staff, are often more enforceable and more directly useful than a sweeping ban on competition. And treating your most valuable information as a genuine trade secret, by limiting who can access it and being deliberate about how it is handled, protects it in a way no signature alone can.

The through line is that these agreements are only as good as how carefully they are written and how well they fit the law where you operate. A form pulled off the internet may be too weak to help or too broad to hold, and either way it can leave you exposed at the very moment you were counting on it. Because enforceability turns on details and on rules that differ from place to place and continue to evolve, this is an area where generic templates are especially risky and tailored guidance is especially valuable.
