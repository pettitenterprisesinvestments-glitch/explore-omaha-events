Twenty-two years in, Robert told his wife Anne that he felt like they were roommates. He said it quietly, almost ashamed, over a Tuesday night dinner. Anne didn't cry or get defensive. She just nodded slowly and said, "I've felt it too. I just didn't know how to say it."

That conversation could have been the beginning of the end. Instead, it was the beginning of something new.

Here's what nobody tells you when you're young and in love: the spark, the giddy, can't-keep-your-hands-off-each-other feeling, was never meant to last forever at full intensity. It's a launch sequence, not a cruising altitude. The couples who stay deeply connected aren't the ones who got luckier with chemistry. They're the ones who learned to *generate* the spark on purpose once it stopped showing up for free.

Robert and Anne started small. They agreed to do one new thing together every month, anything, as long as it was new. A cooking class where they burned the risotto. A hiking trail neither had walked. A terrible community theater production they couldn't stop laughing about afterward. Novelty, it turns out, is one of the few things that reliably reawakens that old electricity. Your brain pays attention to your partner again when you're both slightly out of your depth.

They also did something braver. They started being curious about each other again, as if they'd just met. Anne realized she'd stopped asking Robert real questions years ago. She thought she already knew all his answers. She didn't. People keep growing, quietly, even inside a long marriage. The person across the table at year twenty-two is not the person you married. That's not a tragedy. It's an invitation.

A few things that helped them, and might help you:

- **Trade assumptions for questions.** Ask your partner something you've never asked. What are you most proud of this year? What do you wish we did more of?
- **Flirt again.** It feels silly at first. Do it anyway. A wink, a compliment, a text you'd be embarrassed for the kids to read.
- **Protect time that isn't about logistics.** Most long-married couples only talk about the schedule and the kids. Make room for the conversations you used to have.

The spark didn't come back overnight, and it didn't come back the same. It came back deeper, quieter, more sure of itself. Less fireworks, more hearth fire.

If you feel like roommates, you're not broken and you're not finished. You're just being invited to fall in love again with someone new, who happens to be the same person you already chose.
