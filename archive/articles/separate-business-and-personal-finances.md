Many owners do the hard, smart work of forming an LLC or corporation to put a wall between their business and their personal life, and then quietly knock a hole in that wall without ever noticing. They pay a business expense from their personal account, cover a personal bill from the business account, and run everything through one comfortable, convenient pile of money. It feels harmless. It is not. Mixing business and personal finances is one of the most common ways owners undermine the very protection they paid to create.

To see why, remember what an LLC or corporation actually does. It creates a separate legal person, distinct from you, so that if the business is sued or cannot pay its debts, your personal home, savings, and property are generally shielded. But that shield depends on the business genuinely being separate. When your finances are tangled together, you hand anyone suing your business a powerful argument: that the company was never really a separate entity at all, just you under a different name. Courts have a phrase for what can happen next, piercing the corporate veil, and it means a judge sets aside your liability protection and lets creditors reach your personal assets. The single most cited reason for it is commingling funds.

The fix begins with something simple and almost embarrassingly basic: a dedicated business bank account. Every dollar the business earns goes into it, and every business expense comes out of it. This one habit does more to preserve your liability protection than almost anything else, because it draws a clear, visible line between the company's money and yours. Pairing it with a business credit or debit card used only for business purchases reinforces that line every single day. When the accounts are separate, the story your records tell is unmistakable: this is a real business, run like one.

Keeping the money separate also transforms something owners dread, which is taxes. When business and personal expenses share an account, every tax season becomes an archaeological dig through hundreds of transactions, trying to remember which dinner was a client meeting and which was just dinner. Clean, separate books make it far easier to claim every legitimate deduction, to hand your accountant something usable, and to survive scrutiny if a tax authority ever comes asking. Disorganized, blended records do the opposite, costing you both deductions and credibility.

There is a right way to move money between yourself and your business, and it is not by swiping the business card at the grocery store. When you want to take money out, you pay yourself deliberately, whether as an owner's draw or a salary, in a documented transfer from the business account to your personal one. When you need to put money in, you record it as a contribution or a loan to the business. The mechanics are simple; the discipline is the point. Every clean, recorded transfer reinforces that the business and the owner are two separate parties dealing with each other at arm's length, which is exactly the impression your protection depends on.

None of this requires sophisticated accounting software or a finance background. It requires a decision to treat your business as the separate thing you went to the trouble of creating. Open the separate account, run business money through it and only it, pay yourself on purpose, and keep your records clean. These are small, unglamorous habits, but they are the difference between a liability shield that holds and one that collapses the first time it is tested.
