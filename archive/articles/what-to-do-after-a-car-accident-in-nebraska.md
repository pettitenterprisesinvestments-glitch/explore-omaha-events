No one plans to be in a car accident, but most of us will be in one eventually. In the shaken, adrenaline-filled minutes afterward, it is hard to think clearly, and yet the choices you make right then can shape your health, your insurance claim, and your finances for months. Here is a calm, plain-English guide for Nebraska drivers on what to do.

First, make sure everyone is safe. Check yourself and your passengers for injuries, and if anyone is hurt, call 911 immediately. If the vehicles are drivable and blocking traffic, move them to the shoulder or a nearby lot to prevent another collision. Turn on your hazard lights. Your safety, and the safety of others on the road, comes before anything else.

Next, call the police, even for what looks like a minor crash. In Nebraska, accidents that involve injury, death, or significant property damage must be reported, and an official police report creates a neutral record of what happened. That report can be invaluable later if there is any dispute about who was at fault. When the officer arrives, give honest, factual answers, but be careful not to volunteer blame or speculate. It is fine to say what happened; it is unwise to say the accident was your fault, since fault is a legal conclusion that depends on details you may not know in the moment.

While you wait, document everything. Use your phone to photograph the position of the vehicles, the damage to all cars, the license plates, road conditions, traffic signs, and any visible injuries. Exchange information with the other driver, including names, phone numbers, driver license numbers, license plates, and insurance details. If there are witnesses, politely ask for their names and contact information, because an independent account can make all the difference.

Seek medical attention promptly, even if you feel fine. Some of the most common accident injuries, including whiplash and concussions, do not show their full effects until hours or days later. Getting checked out protects your health and also creates a medical record that connects your injuries to the crash, which matters if you later file a claim. Delaying care can hurt both your recovery and your case.

When you notify your insurance company, stick to the facts. Report the accident promptly, as most policies require, and provide the documentation you gathered. Be cautious if the other driver's insurer contacts you and pushes for a quick recorded statement or a fast settlement. Early settlement offers are often lower than what your claim is truly worth, especially before you know the full extent of any injuries. You are not required to accept the first number, and you are allowed to take time to understand your situation.

It also helps to know the basics of Nebraska law. Nebraska is an at-fault state, which means the driver responsible for the crash is generally responsible for the damages. Nebraska also follows a comparative negligence rule, so compensation can be reduced, or barred, depending on your share of fault, which is exactly why you should avoid casually admitting blame at the scene. There are also legal time limits for filing claims, so do not let things drift indefinitely. Because these rules have important details and exceptions, it is wise to confirm how they apply to your specific situation.
