Few stories capture the spirit of early Omaha as movingly as the founding of Creighton University, an institution born not of ambition but of love and remembrance. In 1878, a school opened its doors on a bluff above the young river city, established through the will of a grieving widow who wished to honor her late husband. That husband was Edward Creighton, a self-made pioneer of the American frontier, and the school created in his memory would grow into one of the great cornerstones of education and medicine in the region.

Edward Creighton's life reads like the very story of the expanding nation. Born in 1820 on a farm in Belmont County, Ohio, into an Irish Catholic family, he had little formal schooling but an abundance of determination. As a young man he found his calling in the rising business of telegraphy, and by the 1850s he had become one of the foremost builders of telegraph lines in the country. In 1856 he married Mary Lucretia Wareham in Dayton, Ohio, and the couple soon made their home in Omaha, then a rough and hopeful settlement on the Missouri River. It was a city very much in the making, as readers can explore in our look at <a href="/article/how-omaha-began">how Omaha began</a>.

Edward's greatest achievement came in the winter of 1860 and 1861, when he surveyed the route of the transcontinental telegraph and supervised construction of the line stretching westward from Omaha toward Salt Lake City. Completed in October of 1861, this line at last joined the Atlantic and Pacific coasts by wire, allowing a message to cross the continent in moments rather than weeks. The achievement helped knit a divided and distant nation together, and it made Edward Creighton a wealthy and respected man. He invested in Omaha with the same energy he brought to the wires, becoming the founding president of the First National Bank of Omaha in 1863 and a leader in ranching, freighting, and commerce. When he died in 1874, he left both a fortune and a deeply held wish that some of it serve the cause of education.

That wish fell to his wife, Mary Lucretia. Though her own life was nearing its close, she remembered her husband's devotion to learning and his faith. In her will she set aside one hundred thousand dollars to establish a free Catholic college in his name. Mary Lucretia died in 1876, and after her passing the responsibility of carrying out her vision was taken up by the family. On the second of September in 1878, Creighton College welcomed its first students, roughly one hundred and twenty young people taught by five Jesuit priests and two lay instructors. True to Mary Lucretia's intent, tuition was free, opening the doors of learning to families of modest means in a way that was remarkable for its day.

The college might have remained a small academy were it not for Edward's younger brother, John A. Creighton, and his wife Sarah Emily, who was herself one of Mary Lucretia's sisters. John was a prosperous merchant and rancher with the same generous instincts that ran through the family, and he devoted much of his fortune to nurturing the young school. Under his patronage the college expanded steadily, gaining new buildings and new ambitions. In 1892 the John A. Creighton Medical College was established, and the institution at last became a university in the fullest sense, joined to a hospital named in Sarah Emily's honor. Over the course of his life John gave roughly two million dollars to the school, and in 1895 Pope Leo XIII recognized his charitable works by naming him a Knight of the Holy Roman Empire. When John died in 1907, the modest college had grown into a true university of several schools.

From those beginnings rose the Creighton University known today, a Jesuit and Catholic institution whose schools of medicine, dentistry, law, nursing, business, and the arts have educated generations and made Omaha a center of healing and scholarship. Its enduring motto and mission, rooted in service to others, echo the very generosity that brought it into being. Visitors strolling the historic streets of the <a href="/article/omaha-old-market">Old Market</a> are never far from a campus whose story began with a widow's tender act of remembrance. In honoring one man's memory, the Creighton family gave Omaha a gift that has never stopped giving, a living monument built not of stone alone but of faith, learning, and love.
