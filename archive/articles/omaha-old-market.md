Ask anyone what to do on a trip to Omaha and the answer almost always begins in the same place: the Old Market. With its brick-paved streets, its restored red-brick warehouses, its flower baskets and string lights and horse-drawn carriages, the Old Market feels like a place that has always been charming. The truth is more surprising, and more inspiring. This beloved district was once a gritty, hardworking wholesale market, and not so long ago it nearly disappeared.

To understand the Old Market, you have to picture Omaha in its booming early decades. As the young city grew into a major hub of the railroad and the stockyards, it needed somewhere to store and move the mountains of goods flowing through it. The result was a dense district of sturdy brick warehouses near the river and the rail lines, where wholesalers, grocers, and produce dealers did the unglamorous work of feeding and supplying a growing region. This was the city's commercial engine room, all loading docks and crates and horse carts, with little thought to beauty.

For decades it thrived. But as the twentieth century wore on and the way America moved goods began to change, the old warehouse district fell on hard times. Business moved elsewhere, buildings emptied, and the once-bustling blocks grew quiet and neglected. By the middle of the century, the area many travelers now consider the soul of Omaha was at real risk of being written off and torn down, the fate that erased so many historic districts in so many American cities.

That it survived at all is thanks to a handful of visionary people, the Mercer family chief among them, who looked at those tired old buildings and saw not blight but possibility. Beginning in the 1960s and gathering momentum through the following decades, they and others began buying up, preserving, and lovingly restoring the historic warehouses rather than demolishing them. Instead of bulldozing the past, they invited artists, shopkeepers, and restaurateurs to breathe new life into it. Slowly, block by block, the gritty market became a vibrant village in the middle of the city.

Today the Old Market is one of the great success stories of American historic preservation, and an absolute joy to visit. Those same brick streets that once rattled with delivery wagons now invite you to stroll past art galleries, boutiques, bookshops, sidewalk cafes, and family-friendly restaurants. The historic passageways and courtyards, full of greenery and twinkling lights, are some of the most romantic and photogenic spots in the entire Midwest. What was nearly lost became the beating heart of the city.

There is a wonderful lesson tucked into those cobblestones: that the things worth keeping are often the things others were ready to throw away, and that a community willing to honor its past can turn it into its brightest future. Omaha could have erased its warehouse district. Instead it saved it, and was rewarded with a downtown that people travel across the country to experience.

So when you plan your trip to Omaha, put the Old Market at the very top of your list. Come walk the brick streets, browse the shops, share a meal, and feel the history under your feet. Whether you are bringing the whole family or planning a special evening out, the Old Market delivers.
