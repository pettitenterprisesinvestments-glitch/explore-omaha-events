Here is a piece of trivia that stumps almost everyone, even lifelong Nebraskans: there is a small city of Iowa that sits on the Omaha side of the Missouri River. You can drive to it from downtown Omaha without ever crossing a bridge into Iowa. How is that possible? The answer is one of the most fascinating quirks of geography in the entire Midwest, and it all comes down to a wild river and a single dramatic night.

For most of its history, the Missouri River has been a restless, shifting thing. Before modern engineering tamed its banks, the river wandered, carving new paths and abandoning old ones whenever the floods came. The border between Nebraska and Iowa was drawn down the middle of the river's channel, which seemed sensible enough, until the river decided to move.

In the spring of 1877, a powerful flood sent the Missouri surging across a great loop in its course. Instead of following the long, curving bend it had taken for ages, the river cut straight across the neck of the loop and forged a new channel. When the waters settled, the old bend had been left behind as a quiet, curved body of water, separated from the main river. Geographers call this kind of feature an oxbow lake, named for its resemblance to the U-shaped collar of an ox yoke. That abandoned bend is the body of water we now call Carter Lake.

Here is where it gets interesting. The land inside that old loop had always been on the Iowa side of the river. When the river jumped its channel overnight, the water moved, but the legal boundary did not. The result was a piece of Iowa now physically attached to the Nebraska shore, sitting right beside Omaha. The matter was argued all the way to the United States Supreme Court, which ruled in 1892 that because the change had been sudden, an event called avulsion rather than the slow creep the law treats differently, the land remained part of Iowa. And so it has stayed to this day.

That is why the city of Carter Lake, Iowa, is the only Iowa community that sits entirely on the west side of the Missouri River, wrapped around the lake that shares its name. It is a living reminder that borders are human ideas, while rivers answer to no one.

Today, Carter Lake is a peaceful and pretty spot, a favorite for boating, fishing, paddling, and lakeside walks just minutes from downtown Omaha and right next to Eppley Airfield. Families come to enjoy the water, watch the planes, and take in a view that comes with a great story attached. It is the kind of place that makes you appreciate how much surprising history is woven into the everyday landscape around Omaha.

The next time someone tells you that you cannot get to Iowa from Nebraska without crossing the river, you will know better, and you will have a wonderful story to tell.
