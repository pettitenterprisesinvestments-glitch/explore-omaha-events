Hiring your first employee is one of the proudest moments in the life of a business. It means you have grown beyond what your own two hands can do, and someone is willing to stake part of their livelihood on what you have built. It is also the moment your legal responsibilities quietly multiply. Becoming an employer is not just a bigger version of being self-employed; it brings a set of obligations many owners do not see coming. Knowing them in advance turns a stressful scramble into an orderly, confident step forward.

The first practical task is to make your business official as an employer in the eyes of the government. That generally starts with obtaining an Employer Identification Number, the federal tax ID that identifies your business much as a Social Security number identifies a person. You will also typically need to register with your state's tax and labor agencies, because hiring an employee creates obligations at both the federal and state level. These registrations are not difficult, but skipping them is the kind of oversight that creates problems later, so they belong at the very top of the checklist.

Next comes a question that sounds simple but carries real weight: is this person actually an employee, or an independent contractor? Owners often assume they can choose, when in fact the law looks at the true nature of the working relationship, including how much control you have over how and when the work is done. Getting this wrong, by treating a true employee as a contractor, is one of the most expensive mistakes a new employer can make, exposing you to back taxes and penalties. Because the stakes are high, it is worth understanding the distinction carefully, a topic explored in our companion piece on the difference between contractors and employees.

Once you have a genuine employee, the paperwork begins, and it is more than a formality. New employees generally complete forms verifying their eligibility to work and their tax withholding, and as an employer you are responsible for keeping certain records and meeting reporting requirements. Many states also require you to report new hires to a designated agency within a set window. None of this is glamorous, but each piece exists for a reason, and handling it correctly from the first hire builds habits that will serve you through every employee who follows.

Then there are the ongoing obligations that come with having people on your payroll. As an employer, you are generally responsible for withholding taxes from wages and paying employer taxes of your own. Most employers are also required to carry workers' compensation coverage, which protects both the employee and you if someone is hurt on the job, and to comply with wage and hour laws covering minimum pay and overtime. Many workplaces must display certain labor law notices where employees can see them. These requirements vary by location and by the size of your business, which is exactly why they are so easy to overlook and so important to get right.

Underneath the compliance checklist lies a simple truth: an employee is a person who is counting on you, and the law reflects the seriousness of that relationship. Treating the obligations as a burden to dodge tends to create exactly the disputes and penalties owners fear. Treating them as the basic responsibilities of being a good employer, and setting them up properly from the start, protects your business, your employee, and the working relationship between you. Done well, this foundation lets you focus on the part that matters most, which is building a team.
