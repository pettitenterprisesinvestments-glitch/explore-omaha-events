Every day, people drive the highways and cross the bridges of Omaha, Nebraska, rarely pausing to wonder where that musical name first came from. The answer reaches back centuries and far up the Missouri River, to a people who still carry the name as their own: the Umonhon, known in English as the Omaha. They are not a footnote to the city's history. They are its first chapter, and a living one. To learn their story is to discover that the word Omaha is itself a quiet testament to courage, movement, and an enduring bond with the land.

In their own Siouan language, the people call themselves Umonhon, a name most often translated as "those going against the wind" or "those going against the current," and more simply as the "upstream people." That meaning is no accident of language; it is a memory of a journey. Tribal tradition and historians alike trace the Omaha's ancestral roots to the lower Ohio Valley, from which their forebears gradually moved westward and northward. Along the way they parted from related peoples such as the Ponca, Osage, Kansa, and Quapaw, and made their way up the Missouri River into what is now northeastern Nebraska, western Iowa, and the surrounding plains. To go against the current was both a literal direction of travel and a fitting description of a determined people. You can sense that same spirit in the way their old earth lodges were built, with the doorway always facing east toward the rising sun, a daily reminder of where they had come from and the long road upstream that had brought them home.

Theirs was a rich and ordered way of life. The Omaha organized themselves into two great halves, the Sky People and the Earth People, each made up of clans with their own duties; the Sky People tended to the tribe's spiritual life, while the Earth People cared for its physical welfare. For much of the year they lived in sturdy, dome-shaped earth lodges in settled villages, farming corn, beans, and squash in the river bottoms. Twice a year they left to hunt bison across the open plains, living in hide tepees on the move. At the heart of their spiritual life stood the Sacred Pole, a cottonwood figure called Umonhonti, "The Real Omaha," revered as a living person and kept in a sacred tent at the center of the village. It united the people as one community and embodied their shared identity, a presence as real to them as kinship itself.

The Omaha have long honored thoughtful leaders. Among the most remembered is Big Elk, a respected principal chief who guided his people through an era of rapid change as settlers pressed westward. His grandson by his daughter, Logan Fontenelle, the son of a French-American fur trader, grew up fluent in both Omaha and English and served as an interpreter in the tribe's dealings with the United States government. In the treaty negotiations of the 1850s, Fontenelle played a central role, and in the Treaty of 1854 the Omaha ceded vast lands while reserving a homeland in present-day Thurston County. His life was cut short in 1855 during a summer buffalo hunt, but his name endures across Nebraska and Iowa in places, parks, and the wooded sanctuary of <a href="/article/omaha-fontenelle-forest">Fontenelle Forest</a> near the city.

It was on Omaha land, ceded under that treaty, that speculators from across the river founded the new city in 1854 and gave it the tribe's name. The bluffs where downtown now stands, the river the city grew along, the very ground beneath its neighborhoods, all of it was long the homeland of the upstream people. Understanding that connection adds depth to any visit and pairs naturally with the larger story of <a href="/article/how-omaha-began">how Omaha began</a>.

The Omaha story did not end with treaties or a founding date. The Omaha Tribe of Nebraska remains a sovereign nation today, with its homeland on the Omaha Reservation and its tribal headquarters in the community of Macy, in the northeastern corner of the state. There, descendants of Big Elk and his people continue to speak their language, hold their annual celebrations, and pass their heritage to new generations. The next time you say the name of the city, remember that it is a gift from a living people, the upstream people who went against the wind, and who are still here.
