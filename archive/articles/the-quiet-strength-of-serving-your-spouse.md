Have you ever lain awake at night wondering if you are the only one still trying in your marriage?

It is one of the quietest aches a husband or wife can carry. You make the coffee, you remember the appointment, you swallow the sharp word you wanted to say, and somewhere underneath it all a small voice keeps a tally: I am the one who gives here. Maybe your spouse feels the very same thing on the other side of the same bed. Two people, each convinced they are carrying more than their share, each waiting for the other to notice. This is where many marriages slowly cool — not in a single dramatic betrayal, but in the daily, unspoken belief that serving has made them a fool.

That belief is worth naming out loud, because it is a lie. The first falsehood that creeps into tired marriages is this: to serve is to lose. We imagine that the spouse who gives the most ends up with the least — overlooked, drained, taken for granted. But the whole of Scripture turns that arithmetic upside down. Jesus, the One with every right to be served, said of Himself that "the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many" (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Mark+10%3A45&version=ESV">Mark 10:45</a>). Service was not His weakness; it was His strength and His glory. When you wash a dish you were not asked to wash, or carry a worry your spouse cannot carry alone, you are not being diminished. You are doing the most Christlike thing a person can do inside a home.

The second falsehood is quieter and sounds more reasonable: service should be even. Fifty-fifty. I will give when they give, and not a step before. It feels only fair, and fairness is not a bad instinct. But marriage was never designed to run on a ledger. "Love keeps no record of wrongs," Paul writes, and we might add that it keeps no record of favors either (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=1+Corinthians+13%3A4-5&version=ESV">1 Corinthians 13:4-5</a>). The moment two people are each waiting for the other to go first, the marriage stalls. Someone has to break the standoff. Someone has to decide that their serving will not be a payment issued only after the other has paid. "Through love serve one another," Scripture says (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/passage/?search=Galatians+5%3A13&version=ESV">Galatians 5:13</a>) — not because your spouse has earned it, but because love, by its very nature, moves first.

Here is the part that surprises tired couples: serving your spouse well is not the same as never being served. A husband who pours himself out and a wife who quietly sacrifices are not building two separate accounts of resentment. They are building one home where giving is the normal weather, where neither has to beg to be noticed because both have decided to do the noticing. This is the strange mathematics of a Christ-centered marriage. The more freely you give, the more you both end up carried.

And let it be said plainly, so that no one in the marriage feels villainized: this is not a sermon for one spouse to wave at the other. Serving is not something you require; it is something you offer. The husband who reads this and thinks of all the ways his wife could do better has missed it entirely, and so has the wife who does the same. The invitation is personal. It begins with the one willing to lay down the scorecard first — and very often, when one person stops keeping count, the other slowly stops too.

So you may still feel, on some long evenings, that you are giving more than you receive. Feelings are honest, and that one deserves a gentle answer. But you are not a fool for serving, and you are very likely not as alone in it as you fear. The God who sees in secret sees every unthanked act of love in your home, and He has promised to return it pressed down and overflowing. Serve your spouse not to win a quiet competition, but because you belong to the One who served you first. Do that long enough, with patience and without a ledger, and you may wake one morning to find what you had been waiting for all along: you were never truly the only one giving. You simply went first.
