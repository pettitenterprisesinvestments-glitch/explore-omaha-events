Getting a letter from the IRS is one of those moments that can make your stomach drop. You see the official envelope, and your mind immediately goes to the worst-case scenario. But here is the truth: most IRS letters are not audits. They are often simple notices about a missing signature, a math error, or a balance that needs attention. The key is knowing what to do next, and doing it calmly.

Step One: Do Not Panic

The first thing to understand is that an IRS letter is not a summons to court. It is a notice, and notices have a process. The IRS sends out millions of these letters every year, and the vast majority are resolved without anything dramatic happening. Take a deep breath, open the envelope, and read every word carefully. Do not ignore it. Ignoring an IRS letter is the only way a small problem becomes a big one.

Step Two: Identify What the Letter Is Actually Saying

Every IRS notice has a letter number, usually printed at the top right corner. It will look something like CP2000, CP504, or Letter 2205. That number tells you exactly what the letter is about. A CP2000, for example, means the IRS thinks there is a mismatch between what you reported and what your employer or bank reported. A CP504 means you have an unpaid balance and the IRS is warning you about possible collection action. Write down the notice number, the tax year it references, and the response deadline.

Step Three: Compare It to Your Records

Before you respond to anything, pull out your tax return for the year in question. Compare the numbers on the letter to the numbers on your return. If the IRS says you forgot to report income, check your W-2s and 1099s. If they say you owe more, look at the math. Sometimes the IRS is wrong. Sometimes you made an honest mistake. Either way, you need to know which one it is before you respond.

Step Four: Respond Before the Deadline

Every IRS letter has a response deadline, usually 30 or 60 days from the date of the letter. That deadline is real. If you miss it, the IRS will assume their version of the facts is correct and move forward accordingly. If you agree with what the letter says, follow the instructions to pay or set up a payment plan. If you disagree, you have the right to respond in writing with your explanation and supporting documents. Keep a copy of everything you send.

Step Five: Get Help If the Numbers Are Big

If the letter is about a small amount, you can probably handle it yourself. But if the IRS is claiming you owe thousands of dollars, or if the letter mentions an audit or a proposed adjustment that could significantly change your tax liability, you need professional help. This is where many families get in trouble. They try to handle a complex tax issue on their own, say the wrong thing, and end up owing more than they should have.

The problem is that hiring a tax attorney or CPA for an IRS dispute can cost hundreds or even thousands of dollars up front. For most families, that is not money they have sitting around. This is exactly the kind of situation where having a legal plan in place ahead of time makes all the difference.

Having access to legal guidance means that when the letter does show up, you are not making decisions out of fear. You can pick up the phone, talk to someone who knows the tax code, and get a clear answer about what to do next.
