Renting a home is the reality for many families in Omaha, and for good reason. It offers flexibility, lower upfront costs, and freedom from the responsibilities of homeownership. But renting also comes with a set of legal rights and responsibilities that many tenants do not fully understand. Whether you are signing your first lease or you have been renting for years, knowing what the law says can help you avoid costly mistakes and protect your family.

Nebraska's Landlord and Tenant Act

In Nebraska, the relationship between landlords and tenants is governed by the Nebraska Residential Landlord and Tenant Act. This law sets out the rights and responsibilities of both parties and applies to most rental agreements in the state. Even if your lease says something different, the law takes precedence. A lease cannot take away rights that the law gives you.

Your Right to a Habitable Home

The most important right you have as a tenant is the right to a livable, safe, and habitable home. This is called the implied warranty of habitability, and it means your landlord is legally required to maintain the property in a condition that is fit for human habitation. This includes working plumbing, safe electrical systems, adequate heating, and a structure that is sound and weatherproofed.

If something essential breaks, like your heat in January or your water supply, your landlord is responsible for fixing it. You should report the problem in writing and keep a copy. If the landlord does not respond, you have legal options, including the right to repair and deduct the cost from your rent under certain conditions, or in serious cases, the right to terminate the lease.

Security Deposits: What You Need to Know

In Nebraska, a landlord can charge a security deposit, but there are rules. The deposit cannot exceed one month's rent for unfurnished units, or one and a half months' rent for furnished units. When you move out, the landlord has 14 days to return your deposit or provide a written itemized list of deductions. If the landlord fails to do so, you may be entitled to recover the full deposit.

Before you move in, document the condition of the unit. Take photos of every room, note any existing damage, and make sure the landlord has a copy. When you move out, do the same thing. This protects you from being charged for damage you did not cause.

Your Right to Privacy

Your landlord cannot enter your home whenever they want. Under Nebraska law, the landlord must give you reasonable notice, typically at least 24 hours, before entering the unit, except in emergencies. Your home is your home, even if you do not own it. The landlord's right to access the property does not override your right to quiet enjoyment of the space.

Eviction: What the Law Requires

A landlord cannot simply change the locks or force you out. In Nebraska, eviction is a legal process that requires the landlord to give you proper notice and, if you do not leave, to file an eviction action in court. You have the right to receive notice of the eviction, the right to a hearing, and the right to present your side of the story before a judge.

The type of notice required depends on the reason for the eviction. For nonpayment of rent, the landlord must give you a seven-day notice to pay or quit. For lease violations, the notice period may be longer. If you receive an eviction notice, do not ignore it. You have a limited time to respond, and missing that window can result in a default judgment against you.

Lease Agreements: Read Before You Sign

Before you sign a lease, read every word. Look for clauses about late fees, maintenance responsibilities, pet policies, early termination, and what happens at the end of the lease term. If you do not understand something, ask. If a clause seems unfair or unclear, that is a sign you need to get advice before signing.

Many tenants sign leases without fully understanding what they are agreeing to, and that leads to problems later. A lease is a binding legal contract, and the terms matter.
