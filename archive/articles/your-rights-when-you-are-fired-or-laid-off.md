Losing your job is one of the most stressful experiences a family can go through. Whether it comes as a surprise or you see it coming, the moment you are told your employment is ending, your mind floods with questions. How will you pay the mortgage? What about health insurance? Did they have the right to do this? The fear is real, but so are your rights. Knowing what the law says can help you protect your family and make smart decisions during a difficult time.

Nebraska Is an At-Will Employment State

The first thing to understand is that Nebraska, like most states, follows the doctrine of at-will employment. This means that either the employer or the employee can end the employment relationship at any time, for almost any reason, or for no reason at all. Your employer does not need a good reason to fire you. They do not need to give you warning. They do not need to prove that you did something wrong.

But there are important limits to at-will employment, and those limits are where your rights begin.

When a Firing Is Illegal

Even in an at-will state, there are reasons for which an employer cannot legally fire you. These are called exceptions to at-will employment, and they fall into several categories.

First, an employer cannot fire you based on a protected characteristic. Federal law prohibits termination because of race, color, religion, sex, national origin, age, or disability. Nebraska law adds additional protections. If you believe you were fired because of one of these characteristics, you may have a wrongful termination claim.

Second, an employer cannot fire you in retaliation for exercising a legal right. If you filed a workers' compensation claim, reported discrimination, took legally protected leave, or refused to do something illegal, and were fired shortly after, that may be retaliation.

Third, an employer cannot fire you in a way that violates an employment contract. If you have a written contract that specifies the terms and duration of your employment, or if your employer's handbook creates implied contractual obligations, those terms must be followed.

Finally, Nebraska recognizes a public policy exception. An employer cannot fire you for doing something that public policy encourages, such as serving on a jury or reporting a safety violation, or for refusing to do something that public policy discourages, such as committing perjury.

Your Right to Final Wages

When your employment ends, you have the right to receive your final paycheck. In Nebraska, if you are fired, your employer must pay you on the next regular payday. If you quit, your employer must pay you on the next regular payday or within two weeks, whichever is earlier. This includes all earned wages, including any accrued vacation pay that your employer's policy says is payable at termination.

If your employer fails to pay your final wages on time, you may be entitled to recover the wages plus a penalty. Do not let this go. Unpaid wages are money your family needs.

Unemployment Insurance Benefits

If you lose your job through no fault of your own, you may be eligible for unemployment insurance benefits through the Nebraska Department of Labor. These benefits provide temporary financial assistance while you look for new work. To qualify, you must have earned enough wages during your base period, be able and available to work, and be actively seeking employment.

If you were fired for cause, your employer may contest your unemployment claim. But the standard for disqualification is specific. You must have been fired for deliberate misconduct connected to your work, not simply for poor performance or a mistake. If your employer says you were fired for cause but the facts do not support it, you have the right to appeal and present your side at a hearing.

Severance Agreements: Read Before You Sign

If your employer offers you a severance package, read it carefully before signing. A severance agreement is a legal contract, and in exchange for the money or benefits being offered, you are typically being asked to give up certain rights. Most severance agreements include a release of claims, meaning you agree not to sue the employer for anything related to your employment or termination. They may also include non-compete clauses, non-disparagement clauses, and confidentiality provisions.

Do not sign a severance agreement on the spot. Take it home, read every word, and get legal advice. You may have room to negotiate, and you need to understand exactly what rights you are giving up before you agree to anything.
