Walk through the doors of the Orpheum Theater in downtown Omaha and you step into another world, a dazzling palace of gold leaf, marble, sparkling crystal chandeliers, and rich red velvet. For nearly a century, this breathtaking theater has been one of Omaha's crown jewels, and its story, from grand opening to near demolition to glorious rebirth, is one of the best in the city.

The Orpheum opened its doors on October 10, 1927, in the golden age of the great American movie and vaudeville palaces. It was built at a cost of around two million dollars, an enormous sum for the time, and no expense was spared in making it spectacular. Nearly three thousand people attended the gala opening night, including the mayor and the famous Knights of Ak-Sar-Ben, all eager to see the lavish new showplace. In an era when theaters were designed to transport audiences into luxury the moment they walked inside, the Orpheum delivered, with soaring ceilings, ornate carvings, and an atmosphere of pure elegance.

For decades the Orpheum hosted vaudeville acts, movies, and live performances, becoming a beloved gathering place for generations of Omahans. But like so many grand old theaters across America, it eventually fell on hard times. By the early 1970s, the building's future was in doubt, and a treasure that had taken sixteen months and a fortune to build was at risk of being lost forever.

This is where Omaha's love for its own history saved the day. In 1973, the Knights of Ak-Sar-Ben, the same civic organization that had celebrated the theater's opening, stepped in and purchased the building. They invested about two million dollars to restore it to its former glory, and then made a generous gift of the theater to the city, on the condition that it be kept as a home for plays, musicals, and the performing arts. In 1975, the beautifully restored Orpheum reopened, rescued from the wrecking ball and given a brilliant new life.

The city's commitment did not stop there. In 2001, a major ten-million-dollar renovation further restored and modernized the theater, ensuring that this historic palace could continue dazzling audiences for generations to come. Today, the Orpheum is operated as part of Omaha Performing Arts, and it remains one of the premier venues in the region, regularly hosting touring Broadway productions, concerts, ballet, opera, and more.

What makes the Orpheum so special is that it is not a museum piece behind a velvet rope. It is a living, working theater where you can buy a ticket, settle into a plush seat beneath a glittering chandelier, and watch a world-class show in a space dripping with history and beauty. There is nothing quite like seeing a Broadway musical inside a genuine 1920s palace, surrounded by the same gilded splendor that wowed audiences nearly a hundred years ago. For a special night out, a date, or a family celebration, it is hard to beat.

The Orpheum is also a wonderful symbol of what makes Omaha such a rewarding place to visit. This is a city that treasures its past and refuses to let its finest things slip away. Time and again, Omaha has chosen to save and restore its landmarks rather than tear them down, and visitors get to enjoy the rich, beautiful results.

So when you plan a trip to Omaha, treat yourself to a show at the Orpheum Theater, or at the very least, admire its magnificent facade in the heart of downtown.
