When you bring on help for your business, you face a deceptively simple-looking question: is this person an employee or an independent contractor? Many owners treat it as a matter of preference, or of paperwork, and quietly default to calling everyone a contractor because it seems cheaper and easier. But this is not a label you get to choose freely, and getting it wrong is one of the more expensive mistakes a small business can make.

Start with what the two categories actually mean. An employee works under your direction and control. You set their hours, tell them how to do the work, provide the tools, and integrate them into the ongoing life of your business. In exchange, you take on real obligations: withholding and paying certain taxes, often providing benefits, and following a long list of labor protections. An independent contractor, by contrast, is essentially a separate business you have hired to deliver a result. They control how the work gets done, usually serve multiple clients, supply their own tools, and handle their own taxes. You pay them for the outcome and stay out of the how.

The reason the distinction matters so much is that it changes who bears the cost and the risk. With an employee, your business carries payroll taxes, unemployment and workers' compensation obligations, and exposure to a range of employment laws. With a genuine contractor, much of that shifts off your plate. That is exactly why the temptation runs in one direction: it is cheaper, in the short term, to call someone a contractor. And that is exactly why governments scrutinize the classification so closely, because the cheaper choice for you is often a loss of protection for the worker and a loss of revenue for the tax authorities.

Here is the crucial point that trips up so many owners: you do not decide the classification simply by what you call it or what your agreement says. The label on the contract does not control. What controls is the actual reality of the relationship, measured by tests that look at how much control you exercise over the worker, how financially independent they truly are, and how integrated they are into your business. If you control the day-to-day work of someone you are calling a contractor, a court or agency may decide they were an employee all along, no matter what the paperwork said.

Consider a common scenario. A growing business hires a worker, calls them a contractor, pays them without withholding taxes, and directs their daily schedule, supervises their work, and relies on them full time for years. On paper they are a contractor. In reality they look exactly like an employee. If that worker later files for unemployment, gets injured, or reports the arrangement, the business can suddenly face back taxes, penalties, unpaid overtime, and more, all at once. Misclassification does not usually announce itself gently; it tends to arrive as a bill.

So what should a careful owner actually do? First, classify based on the real relationship, not the label you would prefer. Honestly assess how much control you exercise; the more you direct the work, the more the person looks like an employee. Second, if someone truly is a contractor, treat them like one: let them control their methods, use a clear written agreement that reflects genuine independence, and avoid blurring the line by managing them like staff. Third, keep clean records of the arrangement. Fourth, when you are unsure, get advice before you hire, not after a claim lands, because the cost of asking the question early is a tiny fraction of the cost of answering it in front of an auditor.

The deeper principle is that worker classification is not a formality to rush past. It quietly determines your taxes, your liability, and your legal exposure, and it is one of the easiest places for a well-meaning owner to create a serious problem without ever realizing it.
