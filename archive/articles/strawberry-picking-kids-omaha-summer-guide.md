# Strawberry Picking with Kids: The Ultimate Omaha Summer Activity Guide

It's June 19th, and you have exactly **two days left** to take your family strawberry picking this season.

If that sentence made you sit up a little straighter, good. Nelson Produce Farm's beloved Strawberry Festival closes **June 21st**—and it's one of those experiences that looks simple on the surface but actually hits all the marks of what summer with kids is supposed to feel like: getting outside, getting your hands dirty, making memories, and coming home with something delicious to show for it.

But strawberry picking isn't just about the strawberries. It's the anchor for a whole day of summer activities that keeps your family busy, happy, and actually tired by bedtime (the holy grail of summer parenting).

Let's break down how to do this right.

## Why Strawberry Picking Actually Matters for Summer

Before you think, "Wait, it's just picking berries"—hear me out.

Strawberry picking does something specific: it gives kids a **sense of purpose**. They're not just being entertained. They're gathering food, deciding which berries are ripe, filling their own bucket, and solving the mild puzzle of "how do I pick this without squishing it?"

It's also one of the rare activities where a 3-year-old and a 10-year-old have equally useful jobs. Nobody's bored waiting for someone else. Everyone's picking.

Plus, there's something almost meditative about it. In a summer filled with screen time, sensory overload, and overscheduled activities, strawberry picking is **slow and intentional**.

And—let's be honest—there's nothing like fresh strawberries that your kid picked themselves. They'll actually eat vegetables for the rest of the week.

## Nelson Produce Farm: What to Know Before You Go

**Location:** 10505 N 234th St, Valley, NE (just outside Omaha)

**Hours:** Tuesday–Thursday & Sunday, 9am–5pm (Last entry: 4:15pm)

**Best Time to Go:** Early morning (9-10am) beats the afternoon heat. Bring a hat, sunscreen, and water—you'll be in the sun.

**What's Included:** - U-pick strawberries (pricing varies by how many you pick—typically $15-25 for a family basket) - Farm-fresh strawberry treats and jam available for purchase - Hayrides through the property - Photo opportunities throughout the farm - Petting areas with farm animals - Playground and open space for kids to burn energy

**What to Bring:** - Hat and sunscreen (non-negotiable) - Sunglasses - Water bottles - Wear clothes you don't mind getting stained - Closed-toe shoes (easier to walk in rows) - A small cooler with ice to keep berries fresh on the drive home

**Pro Tip:** The berries will be slightly cooler and less picked-over first thing in the morning. If you can't go early, go on a weekday rather than weekend.

## Turn It Into a Full Summer Day: Before, During, and After

Here's how to build strawberry picking into a complete summer memory, not just a pit stop:

### Morning (Before the Farm)

Start with a simple breakfast at home—nothing heavy. Then give kids a **mission briefing**: today you're going to become "strawberry hunters" and bring home berries for something special.

On the drive, play a podcast or audiobook episode together (something that makes them *think*, not just zone out—age-appropriate storytelling works great). This builds anticipation and actually engages their brains.

### Mid-Morning (Strawberry Picking)

Plan for **1.5 to 2 hours at the farm**. Pick for 45 minutes to an hour, then let them explore the petting areas and playground for the second half.

Take photos—not just of the berries, but of their faces, their hands, the field. These become the memories.

### Early Afternoon (After the Farm)

Stop for a simple lunch on the way home (or grab a farm treat). Let kids **choose one thing** they want to make with the strawberries—jam, shortcake, smoothies, chocolate-dipped berries. They're more invested if they choose.

### Late Afternoon (The Activity)

Make whatever they chose. If it's jam, they can help mash, stir, and taste-test. If it's shortcake or smoothies, same thing. This turns the strawberries from "we picked them" to "we made something with them."

## What Else to Do This Summer in Omaha (Nearby)

Since you're in Valley already, and you want to build a real summer plan, here are activities that pair well with farm trips:

- **Henry Doorly Zoo & Aquarium** (20 minutes away): The Kiewit Luminarium is incredible for STEM learning, and the zoo itself is world-class. Go once a month all summer. - **Bob Kerrey Pedestrian Bridge** (Omaha proper): Free, scenic, perfect for a family bike ride or walk. Good for clearing your head after a full day.

- **Omaha Central Library Summer Programming** (Free): Story time, STEM workshops, maker classes. Schedule one library visit per week in summer.

- **Scatter Joy Acres Petting Zoo** (Also in the Valley area): Another farm activity if your kids loved the animals at Nelson. Great for a less time-intensive outing.

## One More Thing: Build a Summer Rhythm

Don't try to do everything. Pick **three or four anchor activities** that repeat through summer:

1. One farm visit per month (strawberries now, tomatoes later, etc.) 2. One library visit per week 3. One big activity (zoo, museum, theater) every two weeks 4. One free outdoor thing weekly (bridge walk, creek exploration, park day)

This rhythm keeps summer from feeling chaotic while still hitting that "I did SO much" feeling by August.

## The Real Win

Here's what actually happens: In three weeks, your kids won't remember every detail of strawberry picking. But they'll remember:

- The taste of a berry they picked themselves - You being present and patient while they figured out how to pick them - Laughing together - Making something together afterward

That's the summer memory. That's the win.

**Nelson Produce Farm's Strawberry Festival closes June 21st.** If you haven't taken your family yet, this weekend is it. Go tomorrow morning before it gets hot. Bring a hat, sunscreen, and an open schedule. Make it matter.
