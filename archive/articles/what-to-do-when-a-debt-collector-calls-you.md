A call from a debt collector is one of the most unsettling experiences a family can go through. The phone rings, and suddenly someone on the other end is demanding money, using pressure tactics, and making you feel like you have no options. For many people, the instinct is either to panic and agree to pay whatever they ask, or to ignore the calls entirely and hope they stop. Both of those responses are mistakes. The truth is that you have significant legal rights when it comes to debt collection, and knowing them can change the entire conversation.

The Law That Protects You

The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act, or FDCPA, is a federal law that sets the rules for how debt collectors can and cannot behave. It applies to third-party debt collectors, meaning companies that collect debts on behalf of others, not to the original creditor collecting their own debt. Under this law, debt collectors are prohibited from using abusive, unfair, or deceptive practices to collect from you. If a collector violates these rules, you have the right to sue them, and they can be required to pay you damages.

What a Debt Collector Cannot Do

Understanding the specific prohibitions under the FDCPA is the first step in protecting yourself. A debt collector cannot call you before 8 in the morning or after 9 at night unless you give them permission. They cannot call you at work if you tell them your employer does not allow personal calls. They cannot use profanity, make threats of violence, or publish your name on a list of people who owe money. They cannot lie about the amount you owe, falsely claim to be an attorney, or threaten you with arrest. In the United States, you cannot be jailed for failing to pay a civil debt, and any collector who says otherwise is breaking the law.

They also cannot contact third parties about your debt. They can call a family member or neighbor once, and only to get your contact information. They cannot tell those people that you owe money.

What to Do When the Call Comes

When a debt collector calls, the most important thing is to stay calm and not make any commitments on the spot. Do not agree to pay anything, do not acknowledge the debt as yours, and do not give out your bank account or credit card information. You are not obligated to make a payment during that first call, no matter how much pressure they apply.

Ask for the collector's name, the name of their company, their mailing address, and the phone number. Ask for the name of the original creditor and the amount they say you owe. Write all of this down. Then tell them you need to verify the debt and that you will contact them in writing. End the call.

Request Debt Validation in Writing

Within 30 days of the first contact, you have the right to request debt validation. This means you send a written letter asking the collector to prove that the debt is legitimate, that they have the legal right to collect it, and that the amount is correct. Send this letter by certified mail with a return receipt so you have proof they received it. While the collector is validating the debt, they are required to stop all collection efforts. If they cannot validate the debt, they cannot continue to collect it.

Many debts are sold and resold, and records get lost. It is not uncommon for a collector to be unable to validate a debt, especially if it is old. If that happens, the collection efforts must stop.

Know the Statute of Limitations

Every state has a statute of limitations on debt, which is the legal time limit for a creditor to sue you. In Nebraska, the statute of limitations on most written contracts is five years. After that time, the debt is still legally owed, but the creditor cannot take you to court to force payment. If a collector threatens to sue you on a debt that is past the statute of limitations, that is a violation of the FDCPA.

Be careful here. Making a payment, or even acknowledging the debt in writing, can restart the statute of limitations clock. This is why you should never make a payment or acknowledge a debt without first understanding your rights.

When to Get Help

If a debt collector is harassing you, calling repeatedly, contacting your family, or making threats, you need legal help. The same is true if you are unsure whether a debt is legitimate, if the amount seems wrong, or if you are being sued.
