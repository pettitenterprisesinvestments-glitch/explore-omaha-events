<p>Dear tired parent,</p>

<p>If you have read these words after a day that did not go the way you planned, I want you to hear something before you do anything else: you are doing better than you think. The very fact that you worry about whether you are a good parent is itself a quiet kind of evidence that you are one. Careless people do not lie awake wondering. The truth I want to gently press into your hands today is that faithfulness, not perfection, is what raising a child actually requires, and faithfulness is something you are already giving more of than you realize.</p>

<p>We live in a season that loves to measure parents against an impossible standard. Somewhere there is always another family that seems calmer, more organized, more patient, more put together. But the home you are building is not made of curated highlights. It is made of ordinary mornings and forgotten permission slips, of spilled milk and apologies offered after you spoke too sharply. Grace is the thread that holds all of it together, and grace is precisely the thing that a polished image can never teach. Your children are not learning from a flawless parent. They are learning from a real one who keeps showing up, and that is a far greater gift.</p>

<p>Consider first that your children do not need you to be right all the time. They need to watch you make things right when you are wrong. When you kneel down after losing your temper and say, with honesty, that you are sorry, you are not undermining your authority. You are teaching repentance, humility, and the repair of a relationship more powerfully than any lecture ever could. A child who grows up watching a parent own a mistake learns that love does not require pretending. It requires returning. That lesson will steady them through every friendship and marriage and hardship that lies ahead of them.</p>

<p>Consider second that consistency matters more than intensity. The grand gestures we imagine good parents make are rarely what children remember. What they remember is the dependable rhythm of being fed, being noticed, being prayed over, being tucked in. They remember that you were there in the unremarkable middle of the week, not only on the bright occasions. You do not need a perfect day to be a faithful parent. You need to keep showing up to the same small acts of love, day after ordinary day, trusting that God multiplies what is offered in faithfulness. The drip of steady devotion carves deeper than any flood.</p>

<p>Consider third that your weariness is not a verdict against you. Tiredness is the natural weight of carrying responsibility for another life, and it is a weight every devoted parent has felt. The world will tell you that exhaustion means you are failing or that you must find a way to do it all and smile while you do. But there is no shame in being a finite person who needs rest, who needs help, who needs to lean on a strength greater than your own. Even the strongest among us were never meant to parent in our own power alone. Admitting your limits is not weakness. It is wisdom, and it is the beginning of receiving the help you were always meant to have.</p>

<p>Consider fourth that the work you are doing is largely invisible, and invisible work is the most sacred kind. No one applauds the loads of laundry, the budgets stretched thin, the prayers whispered over a sleeping child, the patient answering of the same question for the fourth time. Yet these unseen labors are the soil in which a whole human life takes root. You may not see the harvest for years, and some of it you will only glimpse when your children are grown and gentle with their own. Plant anyway. Faithful planting is never wasted, even when the field looks bare.</p>

<p>So set down the impossible measuring stick. You were never asked to be a perfect parent, and the children entrusted to you were never given to a perfect one. They were given to you, with your particular love and your particular limits, and that was no accident. Be kind to yourself tonight. Forgive yourself for the moment you wish you could take back, and let tomorrow be new. The grace you long to extend to your children is the same grace you are allowed to keep for your own weary heart.</p>

<p>You are not behind. You are not failing. You are a parent who keeps returning to love, and that is the whole of the work. Rest now. The morning, and the mercy it brings, will come again.</p>
