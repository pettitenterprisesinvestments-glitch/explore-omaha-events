Have you ever caught yourself, late in the night, replaying your spouse's doubts word for word, measuring your entire worth against whether the person lying beside you believes in you?

It is a strange and tender thing to confess, but most of us are guilty of it without ever noticing: somewhere along the way, we made our spouse into a small god. We did not mean to. No one stands at the altar deciding to worship the person they are marrying. But we were made with a deep hunger to be known and approved, and the one who shares our bed and our name slowly becomes the one whose verdict we crave above every other. Their belief becomes the air we breathe, and their doubt becomes our day of judgment. We would never once call it worship. But when a single human being's opinion can hand you life one evening and crush you the next, when their approval alone decides whether you feel like a success or a failure, you have quietly built a throne inside your heart and seated a person on it.

For the one chasing a dream their spouse cannot yet see, this idol is especially cruel. You labor all day, and the work itself does not break you. What breaks you is carrying it home and watching it weighed on the only scale you have learned to trust, the look on their face. If they doubt it, you doubt yourself. If they go cold to it, something in you goes cold too. You have handed them a power that was never theirs to hold, the power to define you, and so their every fear lands like a sentence read over your soul.

Here is the first lie hiding underneath all of it: if my spouse would only approve of me, only believe in me, then I would finally feel secure. But this is the cruelest illusion the idol tells, because no human approval was ever built to carry the weight of your identity. Even if your spouse stood and applauded everything you did, it would not be enough, because the ache you are trying to fill is God-shaped, and only He fits it. Scripture asks the question plainly: "Am I now seeking the approval of man, or of God? If I were still trying to please man, I would not be a servant of Christ" (Galatians 1:10). And it tells us where this road ends: "The fear of man lays a snare, but whoever trusts in the Lord is safe" (Proverbs 29:25). The snare is real. You can grow so busy chasing your spouse's approval that you never once stop to receive God's.

And here is the second lie, the one that quietly runs your days: that your spouse's thoughts about you are the truth about you, and that your success is somehow tied to what they believe. But their thoughts are not sovereign. A frightened spouse's doubt does not author your identity, and it most certainly does not hold the power to decide how far you are able to go. Only God holds that pen. "For I know the plans I have for you, declares the Lord, plans for welfare and not for evil, to give you a future and a hope" (Jeremiah 29:11). Read that slowly. The plans for your life were drawn up by God, not by the most anxious person at your dinner table. "The Lord sees not as man sees; man looks on the outward appearance, but the Lord looks on the heart" (1 Samuel 16:7). The One whose opinion actually determines your worth has already looked at you, all the way down, and called you His own.

So the real work of this season is not to argue your spouse into believing in you. It is quieter, and far more personal, than that. It is to walk into your own heart, find the throne where you seated them, and gently help them down from it, not because you love them any less, but because you have been asking them to be a god, and that is a weight no husband or wife was ever built to carry. When you stop needing your spouse to be your God, two things happen at once. You are finally free, because your identity no longer rises and falls with their mood. And your marriage is freed too, because your spouse is released from an impossible role they were never qualified for, and can simply be your partner again instead of your judge.

There is a kind of peace that comes only when your approval is settled in heaven. When you truly believe the Lord has good and successful plans for you, a discouraging word over dinner stops being a verdict and becomes just a word, spoken by a tired and frightened person you love. You can keep building, not because your spouse finally clapped, but because the God who called you has not changed His mind. You can receive their fear with compassion instead of collapse. And you can go to your knees each morning and hear the only approval that was ever strong enough to hold you.

So, have you been lying awake, measuring your worth by whether your spouse believes in you? Come down off that scale. The approval your soul has been aching for was never theirs to give, and they were never meant to sit on that throne. The seat belongs to God, and He has already spoken His verdict over you, and it is good. Take your worth back from the person you love, hand it to the One it always belonged to, and watch how much more freely you are able to love them once they are no longer carrying the weight of being your god.
