Being pulled over is one of the most common ways that regular, law-abiding people come into contact with law enforcement. It can happen to anyone, and it is almost always stressful. Your heart rate goes up, your mind races, and it is easy to say or do something you later regret. The best way to handle a traffic stop is to know your rights ahead of time, stay calm, and make smart decisions in the moment.

This is not about being difficult or confrontational. It is about knowing what the law says so that you can protect yourself and your family while still being respectful and cooperative.

What to Do When You See the Lights

When you see flashing lights behind you, pull over to the right side of the road as safely and quickly as you can. Turn off your engine, roll down your driver side window, and put your hands on the steering wheel where the officer can see them. If it is dark, turn on your interior light. These are not legal requirements, but they are practical steps that help the officer feel safe, and a calm officer makes for a smoother stop.

Wait for the officer to approach before you start reaching for anything. Your license, registration, and proof of insurance are what you will need, but do not go digging for them until the officer asks. Sudden movements can be misinterpreted.

What You Are Required to Do

In Nebraska, you are required to show your driver's license, vehicle registration, and proof of insurance when asked by a law enforcement officer during a traffic stop. You are also required to step out of the vehicle if the officer asks you to. These are not optional. Refusing to comply with these basic requests will only make the situation worse and can lead to additional charges.

What You Are Not Required to Do

Here is where many people get confused. You are not required to answer questions beyond providing your identification and vehicle documents. If the officer asks where you are going, where you are coming from, or whether you know why you were pulled over, you have the right to remain silent. You can politely say, "I prefer not to answer questions without an attorney present." You do not have to consent to a search of your vehicle.

If the officer asks to search your car, you can say no. Be polite but firm. Say, "I do not consent to a search of my vehicle." If the officer searches anyway, do not resist physically. The fact that you did not consent will matter later if the case goes to court.

Can the Officer Search Your Car Without Your Permission?

In general, an officer needs one of three things to search your vehicle without your consent: probable cause, a search warrant, or a lawful arrest. Probable cause means the officer has a specific, articulable reason to believe that evidence of a crime is in the car. This could be the smell of something illegal, something visible in plain view, or a drug-sniffing dog alerting. If none of those apply and you do not give consent, the search may be illegal, and any evidence found could potentially be thrown out in court.

What About Passengers?

If you have family members in the car, they also have rights. Passengers are generally free to ask if they are being detained and, if not, whether they can leave. In practice, during a traffic stop, it is usually best for everyone to remain calm, keep their hands visible, and follow instructions. If an officer asks a passenger for identification, Nebraska law does not require passengers to provide ID unless they are being lawfully detained or arrested, but refusing can prolong the stop.

What to Do If You Feel Your Rights Were Violated

Do not argue with the officer on the side of the road. That is not the place to make your case. If you believe your rights were violated, the time to address it is afterward. Write down everything you remember as soon as you get home: the time, the location, the officer's name and badge number, what was said, and what happened. If you have a dash camera, save the footage.

If you received a citation, you have the right to contest it in court. If you believe the stop itself was unlawful, that is a legal issue that may require professional help to address properly.
