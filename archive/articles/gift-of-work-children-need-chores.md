<p>Dear parent, there is a temptation that visits nearly every loving home, and it is one of the most disguised dangers of all. It is the desire to give our children an easy life. We work hard so they will not have to. We smooth the path so their feet will not stumble. We carry their burdens because we cannot bear to watch them strain. The impulse is tender and good, but if we are not careful, it can quietly rob our children of one of the greatest gifts we have to offer them: the dignity of meaningful work.</p>

<p>I want to gently make the case that your children need chores, responsibility, and honest labor far more than they need comfort. Not because a tidy house matters more than their happiness, but because the very act of contributing to a family is how a child learns who they are and what they are capable of becoming. A child who is never asked to help grows up believing the world exists to serve them. A child who is invited into the work of the home grows up knowing they are needed, and there are few feelings more steadying to a young heart than the sense of being genuinely needed.</p>

<p>Consider first that work teaches a child competence, and competence is the soil in which real confidence grows. We often try to build our children up with praise, telling them how wonderful and clever and capable they are. But words alone cannot give a child the quiet assurance that comes from actually doing a hard thing and finishing it. When a child learns to make their own bed, wash a dish until it shines, or rake a yard from end to end, something happens inside them that no compliment can manufacture. They discover that they can. That discovery, repeated over years, becomes the bedrock of a person who believes they can face what life brings.</p>

<p>Second, shared work knits a family together in a way that shared entertainment never will. When everyone in a home contributes, the burdens grow lighter and the bonds grow stronger. A child who folds laundry beside a mother, or carries groceries beside a father, is not merely completing a task. They are absorbing a lesson that words could never teach as well: that a family is a team, that love is something you do and not only something you feel, and that belonging always comes with responsibility. These ordinary moments of working side by side are where some of the deepest conversations happen and where character is passed quietly from one generation to the next.</p>

<p>Third, work cultivates the virtues we most long to see in our children. Patience is learned by sticking with a chore that is dull. Diligence is learned by doing a job well even when no one is watching. Humility is learned in scrubbing a floor, and generosity is learned in serving others without keeping score. The Scriptures remind us that whatever we do, we are to work at it with all our heart, and this is a truth a child can begin to live long before they can explain it. A home where work is honored becomes a quiet classroom in faithfulness, and the lessons learned there will outlast every toy and screen.</p>

<p>I know the resistance you may feel. It is almost always faster and easier to do the task yourself. The bed you make will be neater, the dishes you wash will be cleaner, and the morning will go more smoothly if you simply take care of it all. But efficiency is not the goal of parenting. Formation is. We are not raising a household that runs well today; we are raising men and women who will run their own households well for decades to come. The few extra minutes it takes to teach a small child to sweep is an investment that pays dividends long after they have left your table.</p>

<p>Begin where they are. A very young child can put toys in a basket and feel the thrill of being a helper. An older child can take on responsibilities that genuinely matter to the running of the home, tasks that would truly be missed if left undone. Resist the urge to redo their work in front of them, and resist the urge to pay for every small contribution, for some things we do simply because we are part of the family. Let them feel the weight and the worth of being counted on.</p>

<p>One day your children will leave your home, and they will not remember whether the chores were done perfectly. But they will carry within them the steady knowledge that they are capable, that their hands can build and mend and serve, and that work is not a punishment but a privilege. So do not be afraid to ask much of them. In asking, you are not taking away their childhood. You are handing them the tools to build a good and faithful life. With great hope for you and your family.</p>
