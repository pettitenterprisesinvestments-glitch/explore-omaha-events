Every evening at 8:45, after the dishes were done and the house finally went quiet, Maria and David used to drift to opposite ends of the couch. One scrolled. One watched something neither of them really cared about. They weren't fighting. They were just... apart, in the same room.

Then one night David put his phone face-down and asked a question he hadn't asked in months: "How was your day, really?" Maria laughed, a little surprised. And then she answered. Really answered. Six minutes later they were still talking, and something in the room had warmed up.

That accidental six minutes became a habit. They started calling it the check-in, and it's now the most protected appointment on their calendar.

Here's why something so small works. Most couples don't drift apart in one dramatic moment. They drift in a thousand quiet ones, the small evenings where nobody asks and nobody tells. A daily check-in interrupts the drift. It's a standing invitation that says, *I still want to know you.*

You don't need a script, but a few questions help when you're tired:

- What was the best part of your day, and the hardest part?
- Is there anything you're worried about that I don't know yet?
- Is there anything you need from me this week?

The rules are simple. Phones down. No problem-solving unless they ask for it. And no using the check-in to launch a complaint you've been saving up. This is connection time, not a performance review.

Some nights the answer is "honestly, nothing happened, I'm just wiped." That counts too. The point isn't the content of any single conversation. It's the accumulation. Couples who feel close almost always have some version of this ritual, even if they've never named it.

If 8:45 doesn't work for you, pick another anchor. Some couples check in over morning coffee. Some do it in the car. One couple I know does it on a short walk around the block after dinner, where the rhythm of walking makes the talking easier.

Whatever stage you're in, newlywed or thirty years deep, the question underneath stays the same: *Do you still see me?* Six minutes a day is a surprisingly powerful way to keep answering yes.

Try it tonight. Put the phone down, turn toward each other, and ask the real version of "how was your day." You might be surprised how much you've been missing.
