At their thirtieth anniversary party, someone asked Frank and Rosa the question every long-married couple gets: "What's your secret?" Rosa smiled and said something that stuck with everyone in the room. "We never assumed we'd stay close. We decided to, over and over."

That word, *decided,* is the whole story.

Here's a quiet truth about long marriages: two people don't naturally grow in the same direction. Left alone, we drift. We pick up new interests, new opinions, new versions of ourselves. One person gets into faith or fitness or a career change; the other doesn't notice until there's a canyon between them. "We just grew apart" is the most common epitaph for a marriage, and it almost always means *we stopped paying attention.*

Frank and Rosa grew apart plenty of times. The difference is they kept noticing, and kept choosing to close the gap.

They did it with what they called "the big questions," conversations most couples only have while dating and then never again. Where do we want to be in five years? What are we each dreaming about lately? What do we want this next chapter to look like? People change, and if you're not regularly re-introducing yourself to your spouse, you can end up married to a stranger you stopped updating your picture of.

A few habits kept them moving in the same direction:

- **Dream out loud, together.** Share the goals rattling around in your head before they quietly diverge. A dream spoken is a dream your partner can join.
- **Take an interest in their new interests.** You don't have to love their hobby. You do have to be curious about why they love it. Curiosity is how you follow someone as they change.
- **Build something shared.** A garden, a trip you're saving for, a tradition, a project. Couples who only share a mortgage drift. Couples who share a *future* stay tethered.
- **Revisit the questions.** What you wanted at thirty isn't what you want at fifty. Keep asking. The conversation is never finished.

The couples who make it to thirty, forty, fifty years aren't the ones who never changed. Everyone changes. They're the ones who kept changing *toward* each other, who treated the marriage as a living thing that needed tending rather than a finished monument.

Growing apart is the default. Growing together is a decision, made a thousand small times across a lifetime.

So ask your partner a big question this week. Not about the schedule or the kids, but about the dream they're quietly carrying. Then keep asking, for the rest of your lives. That, more than anything, is the secret.
