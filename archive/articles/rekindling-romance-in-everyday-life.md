Do you remember when loving each other felt almost effortless — and do you ever lie awake quietly wondering where that couple went?

There is a particular grief that can creep into a long marriage, and it is not the grief of conflict. It is the grief of a distance that arrived so gradually neither of you noticed it coming. Somewhere between the mortgage and the carpool, the late shifts and the laundry that never ends, romance got quietly filed under someday. You still love each other. You are not in crisis. But the spark that once lit up an ordinary room has been buried under a thousand ordinary days, and you have started to feel less like sweethearts and more like two managers running the same household.

Here is the first thing the tired heart believes: that romance takes more than you have left to give — that to rekindle it you would need a weekend away you cannot afford, a babysitter you cannot find, grand gestures and candlelit restaurants and money and time that simply are not there. But romance was never primarily made of grand gestures. It was always made of attention. The couples whose love still glows after decades are rarely the ones with the biggest vacations; they are the ones who never stopped noticing each other in the kitchen. "Enjoy life with the wife whom you love, all the days of your fleeting life," the Scripture says (Ecclesiastes 9:9) — not only on the rare getaway, but all the days, the ordinary ones included.

And the ordinary is exactly where romance is rekindled. It is the goodbye kiss that lasts two seconds longer than necessary instead of the distracted peck on the way out the door. It is the text in the middle of a hard day that says only that you are thinking of them. It is choosing, at the end of the night, to turn toward your spouse instead of toward your phone. It is dancing in the kitchen while the water boils, flirting with the person you have been married to for fifteen years as though you were still trying to win them. None of it costs a dollar. All of it says the same quiet thing: I still choose you, and I am still paying attention.

Here is the second lie, and it is the more dangerous one: that you will get the romance back later — when the kids are grown, when work eases up, when the season finally slows down. But life does not slow down on its own; it only changes shape. The couple that waits for the perfect season to rekindle their love often discovers that the seasons keep coming and the perfect one never quite arrives. Romance is not something you recover someday. It is something you rebuild on purpose, today, in the unglamorous middle of an ordinary week. "Rejoice in the wife of your youth" (Proverbs 5:18) is written in the present tense for a reason.

This is holy work, not a frivolous one. God himself gave us an entire book of Scripture, the Song of Solomon, to celebrate the delight a husband and wife are meant to take in one another. "Arise, my love, my beautiful one, and come away" (Song of Solomon 2:10) is the voice of a God who is not embarrassed by romance but is the one who invented it. To tend the romance in your marriage is to guard something He called good — and your children, always watching, will learn what love is meant to look like from the way you still reach for each other across an ordinary kitchen.

So start small, and start tonight. Put the phone in the other room for twenty minutes. Ask your spouse a question you have not asked them in years. Reach for their hand for no reason at all. Plan nothing elaborate; simply turn toward them on purpose, again and again, the way you did when it was all still new. Romance is not a fire that dies. It is a fire that goes quiet when it stops being fed, and it will catch again the moment you begin to tend it.

So, do you still wonder where that couple went, the one for whom love felt effortless? Look across the room. They did not leave. They are right here, a little more tired and a little more buried under the ordinary, but still the same two people who once could not stop smiling at each other. You do not have to escape your everyday life to find them again. You only have to start rekindling the romance inside it — one lingering kiss, one kitchen dance, one ordinary Tuesday at a time.
