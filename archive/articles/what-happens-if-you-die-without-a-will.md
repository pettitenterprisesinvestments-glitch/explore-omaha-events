## What Happens to Your Family If You Die Without a Will

Most people know they should have a will.

Most people don't have one.

And most people believe — somewhere in the back of their mind — that *their* family will be fine. That their spouse will get everything. That their kids will be taken care of. That the people they love will figure it out.

Here's what actually happens.

---

## 1. What "Intestate" Actually Means

When you die without a will, the law has a word for that: **intestate**.

It means the state — not you — gets to decide what happens to everything you own and everyone you love.

Every state has intestacy laws: a preset formula that determines who gets your assets, who raises your kids, and who manages your estate. These laws were written for the average family. They don't know your family. They don't know your wishes. They don't care that you always intended to leave your mother's ring to your daughter or that your brother-in-law is the last person on earth you'd want near your finances.

When there's no will, the state's formula wins. Every time.

---

## 2. What Happens to Your Home

This is where families get blindsided the most.

If you own your home and you die without a will, what happens next depends on how the property is titled. If you and your spouse own it jointly with right of survivorship, your spouse inherits it automatically. That part works fine.

But if the title is in your name only — or if you're unmarried — the home goes through **probate court**. That process can take months. Sometimes years. And it costs money: court fees, attorney fees, and in some cases, forced sale if the heirs can't agree.

Your family might still be living in that house while a judge decides who legally owns it.

---

## 3. What Happens to Your Money

Bank accounts, retirement accounts, and life insurance policies all have **beneficiary designations** — and those override your will entirely. If you named your spouse as beneficiary on your 401(k), they get it regardless of any other legal situation.

But any money sitting in accounts without a designated beneficiary — a checking account, a savings account, a brokerage account — goes into your estate and gets distributed according to intestacy law.

In Nebraska, that generally means your spouse gets the first portion, and the rest is divided between your spouse and your children. Not all to your spouse. *Split*. Which means your spouse may not have full access to funds they were counting on — not right away, and not without court involvement.

---

## 4. What Happens to Your Children

This is the one that keeps parents up at night — or should.

If both parents die without a will, **a judge decides who raises your children.**

The judge will look at the most qualified relative who steps forward. They'll consider what's in the best interest of the child. They'll do their best.

But they don't know your sister's patience, your best friend's values, or the fact that your own parents are loving but not physically able to raise young kids anymore. They don't know that you and your co-parent had a clear person in mind — you just never wrote it down.

A will is the only legal document that lets you name a guardian for your children. Without it, that decision belongs to a courtroom.

---

## 5. The Myth of "We'll Figure It Out"

Here is what actually happens when families "figure it out" without a will:

Greif turns into disagreement. Disagreement turns into tension. Tension turns into estrangement.

A grandmother and a daughter-in-law both believe they're acting in the children's best interest. A brother who helped with the down payment on a house believes he has a claim. A second spouse and children from a first marriage both have legal rights that collide.

None of these people are bad. They're just human. And when there's no clear instruction from the person who died, love and loss and money create conflict that courts have to settle.

**The people who suffer most in that process are the children.**

---

## 6. What a Basic Estate Plan Actually Includes

A complete estate plan isn't as complicated — or as expensive — as most people assume. A solid basic plan includes:

- **A will** — names your beneficiaries, names a guardian for your children, names an executor to carry out your wishes
- **A durable power of attorney** — names someone to manage your finances if you become incapacitated (not just when you die)
- **A healthcare directive / living will** — tells doctors and family what kind of care you want if you can't speak for yourself
- **Beneficiary designation review** — making sure your retirement accounts, life insurance, and bank accounts all point to the right people

That's it. Four documents. They don't require a complicated trust or a high-net-worth portfolio. They require sitting down with someone who knows what they're doing and actually doing it.

## 7. The Honest Cost of Not Having One

Probate in Nebraska typically costs between **3% and 7% of the estate's value** in fees and court costs. On a $300,000 estate — a modest home and some savings — that's up to $21,000 paid to the process instead of your family.

Add attorney fees for contested matters. Add the time: probate can take 12 to 18 months minimum for complex situations. Add the emotional cost of your family having to fight a legal process while they're grieving.

And then add the thing that can't be measured: the guardian your children ended up with not being the one you would have chosen.

A will costs a few hundred dollars. A legal services membership costs less than a dinner out each month.

The cost of not having one is measured in everything that matters.
