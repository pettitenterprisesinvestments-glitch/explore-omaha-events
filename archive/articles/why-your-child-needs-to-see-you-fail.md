There is a quiet temptation that visits almost every devoted parent: the urge to appear, in front of our children, like we have it all together. We hide the money worry behind a calm face. We swallow the apology we owe. We tuck away the project that flopped, the goal we missed, the mistake we made, because somewhere deep down we believe that a good parent is a parent who does not fail, and that protecting our children means protecting them from ever seeing us fall.

It is a loving instinct, and it is almost entirely wrong.

A child who never sees a parent fail does not grow up believing their parent is perfect. They grow up believing that failure is unspeakable, something to be hidden, something that happens to lesser people than the flawless adults they live with. And then, inevitably, that child fails, because every human being does, and they have no map for what to do next. They have watched you succeed a thousand times and stumble never, so when they stumble, they conclude that something has gone catastrophically wrong with them. The very thing you did to protect them leaves them defenseless.

What children actually need is not a parent who never falls, but a parent who shows them how to get back up. Resilience is not taught in a lecture; it is caught by watching. When your child sees you try something and fail, and then sees you take a breath, learn the lesson, and try again without falling apart, you have handed them something no success could ever teach. You have shown them, in the flesh, that failure is not the end of a story but a chapter in the middle of one. That is a gift more valuable than any trophy you could help them win.

Failing in front of your children also teaches them what humility looks like, which may be the rarest and most needed lesson of all. A parent who can say the words I was wrong, I am sorry, I will try to do better, models a courage that arrogance can never imitate. Children raised by parents who apologize learn to apologize. Children raised by parents who pretend they are never wrong learn to pretend. We are always teaching, and the lesson of a well-handled failure goes deeper than almost anything we say on purpose.

There is even an honesty about life itself in this. We are not meant to be self-sufficient, flawless, in need of nothing. Scripture says it plainly: "a righteous man falls seven times and rises again" (Proverbs 24:16). Notice that the righteous man is not the one who never falls. He is the one who keeps rising. If we hide all our falling from our children, we accidentally teach them that righteousness means never failing, when in truth it means never staying down.

None of this is permission to fall apart in front of your children, to dump your fears on them or make them carry burdens too heavy for their small shoulders. There is a difference between a child witnessing a parent handle failure with steadiness and faith, and a child being made anxious by a parent who is drowning. The goal is not to perform your struggles for them, but simply to stop hiding the ordinary, human, recoverable failures that are a normal part of any honest life, and to let them watch how you respond.

So the next time you fall short in front of your children, the missed goal, the lost temper you have to apologize for, the plan that did not work, resist the old urge to cover it up and project a perfection that was never real anyway. Let them see it. More importantly, let them see what you do next. Let them watch you own it, learn from it, lean on your faith, and rise. One day, when life knocks them down, as it surely will, they will not panic, because they will remember a parent who fell and got back up, and they will know, deep in their bones, that they can do the same.
