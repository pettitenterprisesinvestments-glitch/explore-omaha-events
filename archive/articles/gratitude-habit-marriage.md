A few years ago, Marcus and Dana hit a rough patch. Nothing scandalous, just the slow erosion that creeps into a busy marriage. They'd started to see each other mostly as a list of shortcomings. He noticed every time she left dishes in the sink. She noticed every time he zoned out on his phone. They were both keeping score, and in that game, everybody loses.

A friend suggested something that sounded almost too simple to bother with: every night before sleep, tell each other one thing you appreciated about them that day. Just one. Out loud.

Marcus thought it was corny. Dana wasn't sure she could find something on the harder days. They tried it anyway, mostly because they were tired of the cold war.

The first few nights were awkward. "Thanks for making coffee." "I appreciated you picking up the kids." Small, almost mechanical. But something shifted within a couple of weeks, and it wasn't the saying, it was the *looking.* Because they knew they'd need something to share at bedtime, they started watching for it during the day. They went hunting for the good. And when you go looking for what's right about your partner, you find it everywhere.

This is the quiet power of a gratitude habit. Our brains are wired to notice threats and irritations, the dishes, the tone, the forgotten errand. Left unchecked, that wiring slowly turns the person you love into a collection of annoyances. A daily gratitude practice rewires the lens. You start collecting evidence that you married someone wonderful instead of evidence for the prosecution.

If you want to try it, a few things help:

- **Be specific.** "Thank you for handling the call with my mom so patiently" lands deeper than "thanks for today."
- **Include the small stuff.** The coffee, the laugh, the way they handled a tantrum. Small acknowledged often beats grand ignored.
- **Don't keep score of who said something nicer.** This isn't a competition. It's a climate you're building together.
- **Say it even when you're annoyed.** Especially then. Finding one good thing on a bad day is how you keep a bad day from becoming a bad week.

Marcus says it's the cheapest, easiest thing they've ever done for their marriage, and one of the most powerful. The resentment didn't survive the daily flood of appreciation. You can't stay bitter toward someone you keep thanking.

Tonight, before you fall asleep, turn to your partner and tell them one thing, just one, that you were grateful for today. Then watch what happens over the next month, not just to them, but to what you're able to see.
