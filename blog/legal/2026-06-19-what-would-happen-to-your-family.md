What would happen to your family tonight if you never came home?

Not the emotional part. You already know that answer.

The legal part.

Who gets your accounts? Who decides where your children go? Who makes medical decisions if you are alive but cannot speak? Who controls your business? Who pays your bills while everything gets sorted out?

Most people have never answered those questions on paper. And the terrifying truth is — if you have not — someone else already has the answer waiting. It is called state law. And it does not know you, it does not know your family, and it does not care what you would have wanted.

---

*Unpreparedness is not a personal failure. But it is a personal choice.*

---

FALSE BELIEF #1: "I don't have enough assets to need a will."

This is one of the most common reasons people put off estate planning. And it makes complete sense on the surface. A will feels like something for wealthy people — people with estates, investment portfolios, real estate holdings. If you are living paycheck to paycheck or just getting started, a will can feel like overkill.

Here is the truth. A will is not primarily about money. It is about your children.

If you have minor children and you die without a will, a court — not you, not your family, not your closest friend — decides who raises them. That court follows a legal process. It considers many factors. It does not consider the conversation you had with your sister five years ago about how you would want her to step in. It does not consider that your brother-in-law is not the right fit. It does not know your values, your faith, your parenting philosophy, or your wishes.

You could have $0 in your bank account and still desperately need a will if you have a child.

*A will is not a wealth document. It is a love document.*

---

FALSE BELIEF #2: "My spouse will automatically handle everything."

It feels logical. You are married. Of course your spouse gets everything and handles everything if something happens to you. That is just how it works.

Except in many states, that is not actually how the law works.

In states that follow intestate succession laws — which is every state when there is no will — assets are often divided between a surviving spouse and children according to a legal formula. Depending on the state and the situation, a surviving spouse may not receive full control of the estate. Assets held only in your name may require probate. Bank accounts, retirement accounts, and life insurance policies with no named beneficiary can be tied up in legal proceedings for months.

And here is the part nobody talks about: even if your spouse does receive everything legally, without a healthcare power of attorney in place, they may face legal barriers to making medical decisions on your behalf if you are incapacitated but not deceased. Hospitals follow legal protocols. Good intentions do not override legal documentation.

*What feels automatic is often anything but.*

FALSE BELIEF #3: "Getting legal documents done is expensive and complicated."

This one keeps more families unprotected than any other false belief. And it used to be true.

Traditionally, a complete estate plan — will, living will, healthcare power of attorney, financial power of attorney — required multiple appointments with an estate attorney, document preparation fees, and a bill that could run $1,000 to $3,000 or more depending on the complexity and the market.

For a family already stretched thin, that cost is a real barrier. So it goes on the list. The someday list. The when-we-have-more-money list.
