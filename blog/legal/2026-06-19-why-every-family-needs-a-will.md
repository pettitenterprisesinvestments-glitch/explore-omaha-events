# The Document Most Families Never Write — Until It Is Too Late

Most parents spend enormous energy planning for their children's future. They think about schools, activities, values, savings, and opportunities. They plan birthday parties months in advance and research car seats before the baby arrives.

And then they never write a will.

It is not because they do not care. It is because writing a will means thinking about something nobody wants to think about — what happens to the people you love most if you are no longer here.

But here is the truth. A will is not about death. It is about love. It is the last clear instruction you leave for the people who will have to make decisions during the hardest days of their lives. And without it, those decisions get made by courts, by statutes, and by circumstances — not by you.

---

## What Happens When There Is No Will

When a person dies without a will, the legal term is dying intestate. What that means practically is that state law determines what happens to everything — your assets, your accounts, your property, and in some cases, your children.

Most people assume their spouse gets everything automatically. In many states, that is not true. Assets may be divided between a spouse and children based on a formula that has nothing to do with what the person would have wanted.

If there are minor children and no surviving parent, a court decides who raises them. That court is not required to choose the person you would have chosen. It is required to follow a legal process that considers many factors — none of which include what you would have said if you had written it down.

---

## The Living Will Is Just as Important

A will covers what happens after you are gone. A living will — also called an advance directive — covers what happens if you are incapacitated but still alive.

If you are in a serious accident or a medical emergency and you cannot speak for yourself, someone has to make decisions about your care. Without a living will, that person is making those decisions without guidance, under enormous pressure, and often in a hospital waiting room.

They may not know whether you want to be kept on life support. They may not know your wishes about specific treatments. And they may disagree with other family members, which turns a medical crisis into a family conflict at the worst possible moment.

A living will removes that burden. It is your voice when you cannot speak. It is one of the most important gifts you can give to the people who love you.

---

## Healthcare Power of Attorney and Financial Power of Attorney

Two additional documents that most families do not have:

A *healthcare power of attorney* names the specific person who is authorized to make medical decisions on your behalf if you cannot make them yourself. This is different from a living will — a living will states your wishes, a healthcare POA names who can act on your behalf.

A *financial power of attorney* names the person who can manage your financial affairs — pay bills, access accounts, make financial decisions — if you become incapacitated. Without this, even a spouse may face legal barriers to accessing accounts and managing finances during a crisis.

Together, these four documents — a will, a living will, a healthcare POA, and a financial POA — form a basic legal foundation that every family should have. Most families have none of them.

---

## The Reason Most Families Never Do It

The honest answer is that estate planning has traditionally been expensive and inconvenient. Making an appointment with an estate attorney, gathering documents, going through the process, and paying legal fees has been a significant barrier for most families.

So it goes on the to-do list. And it stays there.

The other reason is emotional. Nobody wants to sit down and think concretely about what happens when they are gone or when they cannot care for their family. It feels morbid. It feels like inviting the worst.

But every day that passes without those documents in place is a day your family is unprotected in a way that has nothing to do with money or circumstances — only with paperwork.

## What You Can Do This Week

You do not have to do everything at once. Here is a simple starting point:

*Step 1: Decide who would raise your children if you and your spouse were both gone.*
Write that name down. Talk to that person. Confirm they are willing. That conversation alone is one of the most important things a parent can do.

*Step 2: Decide who would make medical decisions for you if you could not.*
Again, write it down. Talk to them. Make sure they understand your values and wishes.

*Step 3: Write down your basic wishes regarding life support and end-of-life care.*
You do not need a lawyer for this step. You just need to think it through and document it clearly.

## One More Thing

If you have already done some of this — if you have a will from ten years ago or a power of attorney you signed before your children were born — review it. Life changes. Wills need to reflect current reality: current assets, current family members, current wishes.

A document from ten years ago may name people who are no longer the right choice. It may not account for children born since then. It may not reflect where you live now or what you own now.

Review it. Update it. Make sure it still says what you mean.
